Week 1 Quiz Key: Safety and Fundamentals

Section A: Multiple Choice

  1. B. Disconnect power.
  2. A. Safety glasses.
  3. B. Treat it as potentially energized until verified safe.
  4. A. Load.
  5. B. Disconnect the source and notify the instructor.
  6. A. A broken or incomplete path.
  7. C. An unintended low-resistance path.
  8. B. It helps prevent accidental contact, hidden wiring mistakes, and measurement confusion.

Section B: Matching

  • PPE = B. Personal protective equipment.
  • Open circuit = A. A broken or incomplete path.
  • Load = C. Part that uses electrical energy.
  • Source = D. Battery or approved supply.
  • Return = E. Path back to the source.
  • Short circuit = F. Unintended low-resistance path.

Section C: Short Answer Guidance

  • Accept hazards such as missing PPE, liquids, clutter, damaged wires, exposed conductors, unclear power state, sharp leads, wrong meter lead placement, hot parts, smoke, or odors.
  • Drinks and clutter can cause spills, shorts, tool slips, hidden wiring mistakes, and unreliable measurements.
  • Source provides electrical energy; path carries current; load uses electrical energy; return completes the circuit back to the source.
  • LOTO awareness means students understand that real workplaces control hazardous energy before work begins; they do not remove locks/tags or work on equipment that can unexpectedly energize.
  • An open circuit is an incomplete path that stops current; a short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path that may bypass the load and cause excessive current or heat.
  • Documentation helps the technician remember evidence, communicate clearly, avoid repeating tests, and support safe handoff to the next person.

Section D: Practical Prompt Guidance

  • Possible open-circuit causes: open switch, loose jumper, missing return wire, wrong breadboard row, broken wire, disconnected battery lead, LED not connected in the path.
  • Unsafe stop conditions: unknown power state, hot component, smoke, odor, damaged lead, exposed conductor, missing PPE, liquid on bench, or unclear instructor direction.
  • Safe sequence should include disconnect source, inspect, identify the intended path, make one change, re-inspect, reconnect only after approval or verification, then test.
  • Path labeling: Battery + is source positive; switch and resistor are path/control/current-limiting parts; LED is the load; Battery - is the return.
  • Corrective actions should be specific: remove liquid, put on PPE, replace damaged lead, disconnect power, label source, clear bench, or ask instructor before continuing.

Week 1 Lab 1 Signoff Guidance

Students should identify at least five inspection items and give reasonable corrective actions. The strongest responses include both the hazard and the reason it matters. For example: "Damaged insulation could expose conductor and cause an accidental short; remove the wire from service and report it."

  • Automatic retry: student ignores PPE, touches staged equipment before inspection, or fails to recognize an unclear power state after coaching.
  • Meets expectations: student inspects before touching, identifies hazards, suggests safe corrections, and explains one safety decision clearly.
  • Extension prompt: ask students to sort hazards into personal safety, equipment safety, measurement reliability, and housekeeping.
  • Coaching prompt: ask, "What could happen if this condition is ignored?" and "What is the next safe action?"

Week 2 Key Notes

  • Voltage is electrical push; current is flow; resistance limits flow.
  • Ohm's Law: I = V/R. For 9 V and 1000 ohm, current is 0.009 A or 9 mA.
  • Yellow-violet-red-gold is 47 x 100 = 4700 ohm or 4.7 k ohm, plus or minus 5%.
  • Student labs are low-voltage DC only.

Week 3 Key Notes

  • Resistance and continuity require power off.
  • Black lead normally goes in common.
  • No beep through a fuse usually indicates open fuse or poor probe contact.
  • Negative DC voltage often means the probes are reversed.

Week 4 Key Notes

  • Fuses provide protection and should not be bypassed.
  • Schematics show electrical function, not exact physical placement.
  • Series circuits have one path; parallel circuits have multiple branches.
  • A filled junction dot usually means wires connect.

Week 5 Key Notes

  • Troubleshooting starts by stating the symptom.
  • Make one correction at a time to preserve the evidence trail.
  • A fuse that opens again requires root-cause investigation.
  • Repair tickets should include symptom, tests, expected and actual results, correction, and final verification.

Week 6 Key Notes

  • Strong final evidence includes safe inspection, measurements, continuity checks, function demonstration, and documentation.
  • For 9 V and 470 ohm, current is about 0.019 A or 19 mA.
  • Certificate completion requires safety, DMM, continuity, circuit building, troubleshooting, documentation, and final project demonstration.

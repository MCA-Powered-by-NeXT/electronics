Student Reading: Learn This First

Today the meter starts collecting real evidence. The two main tasks are DC voltage measurement and resistance measurement. They both use the DMM, but they answer different questions and require different circuit states.

Voltage asks, “What electrical difference exists between these two points?” When measuring an approved battery or low-voltage source, the source is powered because voltage is what the source provides. You place the probes across two points and record the reading with V DC.

Resistance asks, “How much does this part or path oppose current?” Resistance is measured with power off because the meter uses its own small test signal. If outside power is present, the reading can be wrong and the meter can be damaged. Powered resistance testing is a stop sign, not a shortcut.

Expected versus measured is a technician habit. A battery label, resistor color code, or instructor value tells you what you expect. The DMM gives what you measured. Those values might be close but not exact. Your job is not to panic; your job is to record, compare, and explain.

Units matter because numbers alone are mystery soup. “9.1” could be volts, ohms, or someone’s lunch rating. “Battery 1 measured 9.1 V DC” is a useful technical note.

Fun fact: a negative sign on a DC voltage reading often means the probes are reversed relative to polarity. The meter is not yelling at you. It is giving direction information. Record it, think, and adjust your interpretation.

Before practice, write two sentences: “Voltage measurement uses a __ circuit state because __.” “Resistance measurement uses a __ circuit state because __.”

Day 2 Purpose

Students practice measurement as evidence. They set up the DMM, measure approved low-voltage sources and disconnected resistors, record units, and explain what the measurement means. Students should learn that a meter reading is not just a number; it is evidence that must be connected to the circuit condition.

Students also compare expected and measured values. A battery may not measure exactly its label value, and a resistor may not measure exactly its color-code value. The technician habit is to record the evidence, compare it to what was expected, and explain whether the result is reasonable for the classroom task.

Safety boundary: Voltage practice uses approved low-voltage classroom sources only. Resistance is measured only with power off and on assigned components or isolated parts.

Learning Targets

  • Measure DC voltage on approved low-voltage classroom sources.
  • Measure resistance only on disconnected or isolated assigned components.
  • Record measurements with number, unit, item, circuit state, and meaning.
  • Compare expected and measured values without jumping to conclusions.
  • Explain the difference between powered voltage measurement and unpowered resistance measurement.
  • Use probe control and safe setup every time.

Voltage Measurement Procedure

  1. Inspect the meter and leads.
  2. Say the task: "I am measuring assigned DC voltage."
  3. Set the meter for DC voltage.
  4. Place black lead in COM and red lead in the voltage/resistance jack.
  5. Confirm the source is an approved low-voltage classroom source.
  6. Identify the two points or terminals before touching probes.
  7. Touch probes to source terminals with controlled probe tips.
  8. Read the display and record the number with V DC.
  9. Note polarity if the meter shows a negative sign.
  10. Write what the reading means for the classroom task.

Resistance Measurement Procedure

  1. Say the task: "I am measuring resistance."
  2. Verify the component is disconnected from power.
  3. Remove or isolate the resistor if directed by the instructor.
  4. Place black lead in COM and red lead in voltage/resistance jack.
  5. Set the meter for resistance.
  6. Touch probes to the resistor leads without touching metal probe tips with fingers.
  7. Wait for a stable reading.
  8. Record the value with Ω, kΩ, or MΩ as shown or taught.
  9. Compare measured value to the expected color-code or labeled value.
  10. Write whether the result is reasonable, unclear, or needs instructor review.

Common Mistakes

  • Measuring resistance while the component is still powered.
  • Using AC voltage setting for a DC battery or classroom source.
  • Recording a reading without units.
  • Thinking a slightly different resistor reading is automatically a bad part.
  • Letting probe tips slip and touch each other.
  • Writing a measurement without naming what was measured.
  • Forgetting to note whether the circuit was powered or unpowered.

Knowledge Check and Exit Ticket

1. Why is resistance measured with power off?
2. Which meter function is used for an assigned DC battery/source?
3. Why should units be recorded?
4. What does expected vs measured mean?
5. What does a negative sign sometimes mean during DC voltage measurement?

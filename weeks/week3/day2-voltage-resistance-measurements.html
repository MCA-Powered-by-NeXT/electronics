Student Reading: Learn This First

Voltage and resistance are different measurements. Voltage asks about electrical difference between two points. Resistance asks about opposition in an unpowered part or path.

For DC voltage, the meter is placed across two points of an approved low-voltage source or trainer. The source is powered because voltage is present. Use the voltage function and correct jacks.

Identify the two points before probing. For a battery, that may be positive and negative terminals. For a trainer, it may be a test point and return. The points must be named in the note.

Resistance is measured with power disconnected. The meter sends its own small test signal, so external power can make the reading wrong or damage the meter.

Use expected versus measured thinking. A label or color code gives expected information. A DMM gives measured evidence. Difference does not automatically mean failure.

Write one voltage note and one resistance note in complete form. Example: “Battery pack measured 6.2 V DC across source terminals.” Example: “R1 measured 986 Ω with power off.”

Lesson Work

  1. Set up the DMM for approved DC voltage measurement.
  2. Measure assigned classroom sources and record V DC with meaning.
  3. Set up the DMM for resistance with power off.
  4. Measure assigned resistors and compare expected versus measured values.
  5. Revise weak notes into complete technician notes.

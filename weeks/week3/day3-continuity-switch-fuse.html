Day 3 Purpose

Continuity testing helps technicians determine whether a path is complete. Students practice only on unpowered components and learn how open and closed conditions appear on a meter. This is the bridge between simple circuits and troubleshooting.

Learning Targets

  • Explain continuity as a complete conductive path.
  • Test wires, switches, and fuses with power disconnected.
  • Interpret continuity beeps or low-resistance readings.
  • Record open and closed results clearly.
  • Connect continuity testing to source-path-load-return tracing.

Continuity Procedure

  1. Verify the part or circuit is disconnected from power.
  2. Set the DMM to continuity or low resistance.
  3. Touch one probe to each end of the path being tested.
  4. Observe beep, display, or resistance value.
  5. Record the result as continuous, open, or unclear.
  6. If the result is unclear, inspect the probes, contact points, and test setup.

Technician Scenario

A training board LED does not turn on. The battery voltage is acceptable. The LED and resistor look correct. A technician can use power-off continuity testing to check whether the path from the switch to the resistor is complete. The test does not prove everything in the circuit is good, but it gives evidence about one section of the path.

Technician Tips

  • Continuity testing is a power-off test.
  • A beep usually means a path exists, but always understand what path is being tested.
  • Do not test through unknown energized circuits.
  • Document switch position when testing a switch.
  • Label the two endpoints you are testing so the result is meaningful.

Knowledge Check and Exit Ticket

1. What does continuity mean?
2. Why must continuity testing be done with power off?
3. How should a closed switch test?
4. What might an open fuse indicate?
Exit ticket: Explain how continuity testing helps find an open path.

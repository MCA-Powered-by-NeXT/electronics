Student Reading: Learn This First

Today is where the Week 2 ideas become a real circuit. You will bring together voltage, resistance, current limiting, LED polarity, source-path-load-return, and documentation. That sounds like a lot, but the circuit is still simple if you slow down and build it in order.

Start by naming the job of each part. The battery or approved source provides voltage. The resistor limits current. The LED is the load that turns electrical energy into light. The wires and breadboard create the path and return. If one of those jobs is missing, the circuit may not work.

LED polarity matters. An LED is not the same in both directions. The anode and cathode must be placed correctly for the intended circuit. If the LED does not light, polarity is one of the first things to check after disconnecting power.

The resistor belongs in series with the LED, meaning current must go through the resistor and then through the LED in the same path. If the resistor is off to the side, in the wrong row, or bypassed by a jumper, it is not protecting the LED. A resistor that is nearby but not connected correctly is basically a motivational poster: nice to look at, not doing the job.

Measure and observe like a technician. A working LED is useful, but your note should say more than “it worked.” Record battery voltage if measured, resistor value, LED polarity check, pre-power path trace, result, and any correction.

If the LED does not light, do not panic and do not start moving every wire. Disconnect power first. Then trace the loop: source, resistor, LED, return. Change one thing at a time and record what changed. Troubleshooting is not guessing faster; it is thinking slower.

Before building, write this sentence: “My LED circuit should work when current travels from __ through __ through __ and returns to __.”

Day 4 Purpose

Students apply Week 2 concepts by inspecting parts, checking polarity, measuring battery voltage, building a current-limited LED circuit, and documenting the result. This is the first place where voltage, resistance, current limiting, polarity, and safe lab habits all come together.

Safety reminder: Disconnect power before changing wires. Use only instructor-approved low-voltage batteries or training sources.

Learning Targets

  • Measure battery voltage with instructor-approved DMM setup.
  • Identify LED polarity and resistor placement.
  • Build a current-limited LED circuit from a simple plan.
  • Troubleshoot a non-lighting LED using source, path, load, and return reasoning.
  • Document measurements, symptoms, corrections, and final result.

Pre-Power Inspection

  • [ ] Safety glasses on if required by instructor.
  • [ ] Bench is clear of liquids, loose metal, and clutter.
  • [ ] Battery or source polarity is identified.
  • [ ] Resistor is in series with the LED.
  • [ ] LED polarity has been checked.
  • [ ] Source, path, load, and return can be traced.
  • [ ] No jumper bypasses the resistor or LED.

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. Inspect the bench and parts before touching the circuit.
  2. Identify battery positive and negative.
  3. Set up the DMM for DC voltage and measure the battery if directed.
  4. Record the battery voltage with units.
  5. Identify LED anode and cathode.
  6. Place the resistor in series with the LED.
  7. Trace the circuit from source to return before connecting power.
  8. Connect power and observe the LED.
  9. If the LED does not light, disconnect power before changing anything.
  10. Document the symptom, likely cause, correction, and final result.

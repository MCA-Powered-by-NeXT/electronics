Student Reading: Learn This First

Week 1 taught you to see a circuit as a loop: source, path, load, and return. Week 2 adds words that describe what is happening in that loop. The words are voltage, current, resistance, and power. These are not fancy vocabulary words to memorize and forget. They are the labels technicians use to explain circuit behavior.

Voltage is the electrical difference that a source provides. In beginner language, voltage is like the push available from the battery or approved DC source. A 9 V battery is not pushing current through the air by itself; it has voltage available between its two terminals.

Current is the flow of electric charge through a complete path. Current is the thing that actually travels through the circuit path. It can only flow when the loop is complete. If the return path is missing, the circuit is like a racetrack with a missing bridge. The cars are not finishing the lap.

Resistance is opposition to current. Resistance can sound like a problem, but in electronics it is often useful. A resistor can limit current so an LED is not overloaded. Resistance is like the “calm down, buddy” part of the circuit. Without it, some parts may try to take more current than they should.

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is used or changed into another form. An LED changes electrical energy into light. A buzzer changes electrical energy into sound. A resistor may change some electrical energy into heat. Power reminds technicians that parts have limits and jobs.

Fun fact: electricians and electronics technicians use many of the same words, but this course stays in small, low-voltage DC training circuits. That means you can learn the language safely without treating a wall outlet like a science fair volcano. Do not poke the volcano.

Before the activity, write four short sentences: “Voltage is __.” “Current is __.” “Resistance is __.” “Power is __.” Then add the unit for each: V, A, Ω, or W.

Day 1 Purpose

Students connect the Week 1 circuit loop to the quantities that describe circuit behavior. Voltage, current, resistance, and power are introduced as practical technician words. The goal is for students to talk about what is happening in a low-voltage circuit before they calculate anything.

Safety boundary: Discussion and demonstrations use low-voltage battery-powered DC examples only. Students do not probe unknown sources or wall power.

Learning Targets

  • Define voltage, current, resistance, and power in beginner language.
  • Match each quantity to its unit: volts, amps, ohms, and watts.
  • Explain why current requires a complete path.
  • Use source-path-load-return vocabulary while describing voltage, current, and resistance.
  • Write a short technician note that includes a quantity, a unit, and an observation.

Main Concept: Quantities Describe the Circuit

In Week 1, students learned that a working circuit needs a complete loop. In Week 2, students learn that the loop also has measurable behavior. A battery can provide voltage, a complete path can allow current, and resistance can limit how much current flows.

Battery voltage -> current through the intended path -> resistance limits current -> load uses energy -> return completes the loop

Instructor explanation: Do not let students memorize disconnected definitions. Ask them to point to a circuit and say, "Here is the source of voltage, here is the path current would take, here is the resistance, and here is the load."

Guided Demonstration

  1. Display a simple battery-resistor-LED circuit or Tinkercad version.
  2. Ask students to identify the source, path, load, and return.
  3. Label the battery voltage.
  4. Point out the resistor and ask what it is controlling.
  5. Ask what must be true before current can flow.
  6. Have students write one observation using at least one unit.

Common Mistakes

  • Calling voltage current: Voltage is electrical push; current is flow through a complete path.
  • Forgetting the return path: A battery connected on one side is not enough.
  • Writing numbers without units: A technician note must say V, A, Ω, or W when appropriate.
  • Thinking resistance is always bad: Resistance is often intentionally used to protect parts and control current.

Write three sentences about a simple LED circuit. Sentence 1 must use the word voltage. Sentence 2 must use the word current. Sentence 3 must use the word resistance.

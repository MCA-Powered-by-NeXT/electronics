Student Reading: Learn This First

Start with the Week 1 circuit loop. A circuit needs source, path, load, and return. Voltage, current, resistance, and power describe what happens in that loop.

Voltage is electrical potential difference. For beginners, think of it as the electrical push provided by the source. A battery has voltage between its terminals. Later, you will measure voltage across two points.

Current is the movement of electric charge through a complete path. Current does not move through an open path. This is why the return path matters.

Resistance is opposition to current. A resistor provides intentional resistance. Resistance is not always a problem; it is often how a circuit is controlled.

Power describes how quickly electrical energy is converted by a load. An LED converts electrical energy to light. A buzzer converts electrical energy to sound. A resistor may convert electrical energy to heat.

Use units correctly. Voltage uses volts, current uses amperes, resistance uses ohms, and power uses watts. A number without a unit is not a complete technical note.

Write this sentence: “In a battery-LED circuit, the source provides __, current flows only if __, the resistor __, and the LED __.”

Lesson Work

  1. Define voltage, current, resistance, and power in your own words.
  2. Match each quantity to its unit and symbol.
  3. Label where each quantity appears in a simple LED circuit.
  4. Identify approved classroom DC sources and explain why wall outlets are outside the course boundary.

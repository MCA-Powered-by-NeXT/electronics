Week 1: Electrical Safety and Technician Fundamentals

Start the course by learning how technicians think: inspect first, work safely, use clear vocabulary, document what you observe, and simulate circuits before building them.

Week One Purpose

Week One sets the expectations for the entire Basic Electronics Technician course. Before students build circuits, measure voltage, or troubleshoot faults, they need a reliable safety routine, a simple way to describe how circuits work, and a safe place to test ideas before touching physical parts. This week introduces all three.

By the end of the week, students should be able to inspect a workstation, identify hazards, explain why power state matters, use basic technician vocabulary, describe a simple circuit using the source-path-load-return model, and build a simple simulated circuit in Tinkercad Circuits.

Course safety boundary: All Week One physical activities use disconnected training equipment or low-voltage battery-powered DC circuits only. Students must not work on wall outlets, open power supplies, vehicle electrical systems, charged capacitors, or unknown energized equipment. Tinkercad simulation is used to practice circuit thinking before physical builds.

What Students Should Be Ready To Do

Week One is successful when students can slow down, observe carefully, explain their reasoning, and test ideas safely. The goal is not speed. The goal is safe, repeatable habits.

  • Approach a workstation with an inspection mindset before touching parts or tools.
  • Use PPE and explain why it matters, even during low-voltage training activities.
  • Recognize obvious hazards such as clutter, damaged leads, missing PPE, unclear power state, exposed conductors, and liquids near the bench.
  • Explain PPE, ESD awareness, and lockout/tagout awareness at an introductory level.
  • Use the source-path-load-return model to describe a simple circuit.
  • Distinguish open circuits, closed circuits, and short circuits in beginner-friendly language.
  • Use Tinkercad Circuits to place a battery, switch, resistor, LED, and wires in a safe simulated workspace.
  • Write short notes that describe what was observed and what action should be taken.

Required Lessons and Lab

The Week One sequence moves from professional expectations to safe virtual practice, then into the first simple circuit model. Complete the materials in order.

Lesson 1: Safety and Technician Fundamentals

Introduces technician roles, safe work habits, PPE, hazard recognition, ESD awareness, LOTO awareness, and the technician safety loop.

Supplemental Lesson: Making Circuits in Tinkercad

Introduces browser-based circuit simulation, safe virtual experimentation, component placement, simple wiring, and simulation notes.

Lesson 2: Introduction to Electricity

Introduces source, path, load, return, conductors, insulators, open circuits, closed circuits, short circuits, and simple circuit reasoning.

Lab 1: Safety Inspection and PPE

Students inspect a staged workstation, identify hazards, choose PPE, document corrective actions, and receive instructor signoff.

Week One Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts to connect the safety material to real technician work.

  • Why is a careful inspection part of the job, not a delay before the job?
  • What could happen if a technician assumes a circuit is unpowered without checking?
  • Why might a clean, organized bench produce better measurements?
  • How can simulation help a student find wiring mistakes before using physical parts?
  • What is the difference between a circuit that is off because it is open and a circuit that is unsafe because it is shorted?
  • How does documentation protect the technician, the instructor, and the next person who works on the circuit?

Week 1 Quiz and Review

Week 1 Quiz checks hazard recognition, PPE, safe work sequence, circuit vocabulary, and the source-path-load-return model.

Review prompt: Why is "power off first" a better technician habit than "be careful while it is powered"?

Key Takeaways

  • Safety is a repeatable process: inspect, identify hazards, control power, verify, work, test, and document.
  • Beginner labs are low-voltage, but professional habits still matter.
  • Simulation is useful for practicing circuit logic, but it does not replace real-world safety habits.
  • Every working circuit needs a source, path, load, and return.
  • Open circuits stop current; short circuits send current through an unintended path.
  • Clear notes and correct vocabulary make troubleshooting easier later.

What To Do Next

After Week One, students should be ready for Week Two circuit work: measuring batteries, identifying resistor values, applying simple Ohm's Law, and building a current-limited LED circuit.

Instructor Notes

Instructor Only: Stage safe, simulated hazards for Lab 1. Use disconnected props only. Recommended examples include cluttered leads, missing safety glasses, damaged insulation on a sample wire, unlabeled batteries, a cup prop near the bench, and a powered-off meter with leads placed in the wrong jacks as a discussion prop. For the Tinkercad lesson, confirm students can access the tool before class or prepare a projected instructor demonstration.

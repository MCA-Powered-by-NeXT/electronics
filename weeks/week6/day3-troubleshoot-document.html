Week 6 Day 3: Troubleshoot, Revise, Document, and Maintain

2-3 hour class block for correcting project issues, verifying final operation, drafting documentation, and connecting project work to preventive maintenance habits.

Day 3 Purpose

Students use the Week 5 troubleshooting process to correct final project issues and prepare a clear record of build steps, measurements, faults, corrections, and verification. This is the day to turn an incomplete build into a documented technician result.

Students also learn that a finished circuit is not the end of technician work. A useful device or trainer must be inspected, documented, maintained, and handed off so another person can understand its condition.

Safety boundary: Students troubleshoot only their approved low-voltage projects. Power must be disconnected before rewiring, continuity testing, resistance testing, or moving parts.

Learning Targets

  • Identify and correct final project issues safely.
  • Use symptom, expected operation, evidence, correction, and verification language.
  • Apply the Week 5 troubleshooting flow to a student-built project.
  • Verify final project operation using observation, assigned measurement, or power-off continuity evidence.
  • Prepare a final documentation draft that another person could understand.
  • Write one preventive maintenance observation for the project or workstation.
  • Practice the explanation needed for the final demonstration.

Documentation Requirements

  • Project name and purpose.
  • Approved project plan or revised plan.
  • Parts list with source, control, load, return, and protection components.
  • Simple circuit sketch or description.
  • Safety checks completed before power-up.
  • Measurements or tests with units and circuit state.
  • Faults found, evidence collected, corrections made, and verification results.
  • Preventive maintenance observation.
  • Final demonstration script or talking points.
  • What the student would improve next time.

Preventive Maintenance Connection

Preventive maintenance means checking and documenting conditions before failure interrupts work. For this course, students practice preventive maintenance through safe inspection of tools, wires, connectors, batteries, labels, and project condition.

CheckStudent Looks ForExample Maintenance Note
Tool conditionDMM leads intact, no cracked probes, meter stored correctly."DMM leads inspected; no visible damage. Returned to storage."
Wire conditionNo frayed strands, extra exposed conductor, or loose jumpers."Blue jumper insulation nicked; removed from student kit."
Connector seatingBattery clip or connector fully seated and not strained."Battery clip lead strained; rerouted to reduce pull."
LabelingProject source, control, and return labels readable."Added return label to black lead for future troubleshooting."
HousekeepingScraps removed, parts returned, liquids away, bench reset."Station reset and loose resistor returned to parts bin."

Demo Prep Questions

What does your project do?
Where are source, control point, load, path, and return?
What measurement or test proves it works?
What problem did you solve?
What safety habit did you use?
What preventive maintenance note would help the next person?

Common Documentation Problems

  • Only writing "fixed" without explaining what changed.
  • Leaving out units on measurements.
  • Not recording the original symptom.
  • Not verifying after correction.
  • Writing notes that only make sense to the person who built the project.
  • Forgetting to update the sketch after a design revision.
  • Leaving out the safety state of the circuit during testing.

Exit Ticket

State your final project status, one piece of verification evidence, one preventive maintenance observation, and one thing you still need before the demonstration.

Student Reading: Learn This First

Week 5 gives you the troubleshooting habits you will need for the final project. A project that does not work on the first try is not a disaster. It is a troubleshooting opportunity. The key is to respond like a technician instead of like a panicked squirrel.

Start every troubleshooting situation with the symptom. What did you observe? Then describe expected operation. What should the circuit do? The gap between those two statements tells you what problem you are trying to solve.

Review the fault categories: source, control, path, load, return, component value, connector or terminal, and workmanship. These categories help you choose where to look first. They also keep you from blaming the same part every time.

Repair tickets are not busywork. They help you prove what happened and remember what you changed. A good repair ticket includes symptom, safety step, evidence, confirmed cause, correction, and verification. If your final project fails later, your notes help you avoid starting over from zero.

Verification is the final proof. A repair is not finished when you move a wire. A repair is finished when the circuit does what it is supposed to do and you can explain how you know.

Fun fact: many real technicians build a reputation not by never making mistakes, but by documenting clearly, staying safe, and solving problems without creating extra ones. That is the Week 5 goal.

Before the quiz, complete this sentence: “When my circuit does not work, I will first __, then __, then __.”

Beginner Extra Help: Final Project Troubleshooting Starter

If your Week 6 project does not work, do not rebuild the whole thing immediately. Start with three questions: What should happen? What actually happens? What is the safest first check?

Then follow the path. Source, control, load, return. If the project has branches, compare the working branch to the nonworking branch. If the output is intermittent, inspect connectors and wire seating. If the output is weak, check source condition and component values.

Joke check: “I will scream internally and poke wires” is not a troubleshooting plan. It is a feeling. Feelings are allowed. Poking wires while powered is not.

Day 5 Purpose

Students leave Week 5 with a troubleshooting process they can use independently in Week 6. The day includes review, documentation cleanup, a quiz, corrections, and a final project readiness bridge. Students should be able to explain not just "what was wrong" but how they knew it was wrong.

Beginner electronics examples often show simple cause-and-effect: a switch opens, current stops; a wire is missing, the path is open; a resistor value changes, circuit behavior changes. Day 5 uses those simple relationships to help students explain real troubleshooting decisions.

Review Targets

  • Control and workmanship vocabulary: input, output, switch, contact, connector, terminal, intermittent fault.
  • Wire and connector inspection: color, condition, seating, stripped length, routing, strain, polarity.
  • Troubleshooting flow: observe, make safe, inspect, compare, isolate, test, correct, verify, document.
  • Fault categories: source, control, path, load, return, component value, connector/terminal, workmanship.
  • Repair ticket evidence: symptom, safety step, evidence, confirmed cause, correction, verification.
  • Safe low-voltage habits: disconnect before rewiring, continuity only with power off, one change at a time.
  • Final project readiness: plan expected operation, test in stages, document changes, verify before demo.

Documentation Cleanup Workshop

Students choose one repair ticket from Day 3 or Day 4 and revise it. The final note should be short but complete.

Confirmed cause[ ]"The confirmed cause was..."
Correction[ ]"The correction made was..."
Verification[ ]"After correction, the circuit..."
Rewrite this weak note: "LED did not work. Fixed wire. Works now."

Week 5 Quiz

  1. What is the first thing a technician should record when troubleshooting?
  2. Why should a technician change only one thing at a time?
  3. Name six fault categories used this week.
  4. Why does workmanship affect troubleshooting?
  5. What belongs in a complete repair ticket?
  6. What safety action comes before rewiring or continuity testing?
  7. How does source-control-load-return reasoning help fault isolation?
  8. How do you verify that a repair worked?
  9. What is the difference between a symptom, possible cause, evidence, and confirmed cause?
  10. A switch-controlled LED is off. List three possible fault categories and one safe first check for each.
  11. Write a complete repair note for a missing return wire fault.
  12. Explain why wire color is helpful but not enough proof.

Final Project Readiness Checklist

  • [ ] I can draw or describe expected operation before building.
  • [ ] I can identify source, control, load, and return in my project.
  • [ ] I can inspect workmanship before applying power.
  • [ ] I can troubleshoot without guessing.
  • [ ] I can document build steps, symptoms, evidence, corrections, and verification.
  • [ ] I can explain my work to an instructor using technician vocabulary.
  • [ ] I can verify a result instead of assuming the repair worked.
  • [ ] I can ask for help before leaving the low-voltage classroom boundary.

Exit Ticket

Write the first three troubleshooting steps you will use if your Week 6 final project does not work on the first try. Then write one sentence explaining how documentation helps your future self.

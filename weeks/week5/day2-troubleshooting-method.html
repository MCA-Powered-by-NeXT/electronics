Week 5 Day 2: Systematic Troubleshooting Method

2-3 hour class block for troubleshooting without guessing.

Day 2 Purpose

Students learn a repeatable troubleshooting process. The goal is to use symptoms and evidence instead of random part swapping. Students practice describing the problem, choosing safe first checks, and documenting evidence before correction.

Learning Targets

  • Describe a troubleshooting sequence.
  • Separate power, path, load, return, component, and workmanship faults.
  • Choose safe first checks based on the symptom.
  • Explain why one change at a time matters.
  • Write useful repair notes.

Troubleshooting Flow

1. Observe the symptom
2. Make the circuit safe
3. Inspect visible conditions
4. Isolate likely section: source, path, load, return
5. Measure or test one thing
6. Correct one thing
7. Verify the result
8. Document evidence

Symptom vs Cause

A symptom is what the circuit is doing or not doing. A cause is the reason. "LED is off" is a symptom. "Return wire is missing" may be a cause. Technicians avoid guessing the cause until they have evidence.

Repair Note Model

Symptom: LED stayed off when switch was closed.
Safety: Battery disconnected before inspection.
Evidence: Return wire was not connected to battery negative.
Correction: Reconnected return wire.
Verification: LED turned on after reconnecting power.

Common Mistakes

  • Changing parts before describing the symptom.
  • Making several changes and not knowing which one fixed the circuit.
  • Skipping a visual inspection.
  • Measuring the wrong thing because the fault category was not identified.
  • Writing "fixed it" instead of documenting evidence.

Knowledge Check and Exit Ticket

1. Why is guessing a weak troubleshooting method?
2. What is the difference between a symptom and a cause?
3. Why should only one thing be changed at a time?
4. What evidence belongs in a repair ticket?
Exit ticket: Write the troubleshooting flow from memory.

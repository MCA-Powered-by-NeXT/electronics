Week 5 Support: Wire Identification, Connectors, and Terminals

Supplemental classroom content for physical wiring, connector condition, terminal quality, and intermittent faults.

Purpose

This page adds focused coverage for wire identification, connectors, and terminals. These topics support troubleshooting because many beginner faults are physical: a wire is in the wrong row, a connector is not seated, a terminal is loose, or too much bare conductor is exposed.

The uploaded beginner electronics PDF includes early examples of conductors, insulators, wires, cables, switches, relays, and resistors. This support page adapts those ideas into safe classroom inspection tasks using only assigned low-voltage trainers and disconnected samples.

Learning Targets

  • Identify wire characteristics: color, size, insulation condition, solid/stranded type, label, and routing.
  • Identify connector and terminal features that affect reliability.
  • Explain how poor termination can create open, intermittent, or unintended-path faults.
  • Use power-off continuity testing only when it is assigned and appropriate.
  • Write a technician note that describes a wire or connector issue without guessing.

Wire Identification

FeatureWhat To ObserveWhy It MattersClassroom Example
ColorRed, black, blue, yellow, or other insulation colors.Color can organize a circuit, but the actual path must be traced.Red is used for source positive on a trainer, but student still traces it to the correct row.
Size / gaugeRelative thickness or printed size if available.Wire must fit the connection point and carry the expected low-voltage training current.A jumper too thick may damage a breadboard contact; a tiny strand may not hold in a terminal.
Solid or strandedSingle stiff conductor or many fine strands.Solid wire seats well in breadboards; stranded wire is flexible but can fray.Compare one solid jumper and one stranded battery lead.
Insulation conditionCracks, cuts, melted spots, or extra exposed conductor.Damage can expose metal or create unreliable operation.A wire stripped too long can touch a neighboring terminal.
LabelingTags, numbers, written labels, or documented circuit locations.Labels help handoff and troubleshooting."J1 return" label matches a repair ticket note.
Routing and strainWire path is neat, not stretched, pinched, or hiding parts.Strain can pull connectors loose; messy routing hides faults.A battery clip lead is stretched across the board and disconnects when moved.

Connectors and Terminals

ItemBeginner MeaningInspection QuestionsFault It Can Cause
TerminalA point where a wire or component connects.Is the conductor secure? Is extra metal showing?Open path or unintended contact.
ConnectorA removable or fixed part that joins conductors.Is it seated? Is it cracked, loose, reversed, or mismatched?Intermittent output or no source/return.
Screw terminalA terminal tightened by a screw.Is the conductor under the clamp? Are strands outside?Loose connection or frayed strand contact.
Crimp terminalA terminal mechanically attached to a wire.Is the crimp secure? Are strands broken?Connection fails when wire moves.
Breadboard connectionA spring contact inside a breadboard row.Is the wire fully seated in the correct row?Open path, wrong row, or intermittent fault.
Battery clipConnector from training source to circuit.Is polarity correct? Is clip seated and not strained?No source, reversed source, or intermittent source.

Inspection Demonstration

  1. Show students a good wire sample and a poor wire sample side by side.
  2. Ask students to describe what they see without using the word "bad." Require visible evidence.
  3. Show a breadboard jumper that looks inserted but is in the wrong row.
  4. Show a connector that is partly seated and ask what symptom it might create.
  5. Show a switch with three terminals and ask why terminal identification matters.
  6. Show a resistor in the wrong row and ask whether it is actually in the current path.

Connector Fault Examples

SymptomPossible Wiring / Connector CauseSafe First CheckTechnician Note Example
LED flickers when board moves.Loose jumper or terminal.Power off, inspect seating, then continuity check if approved."LED flickered when board moved; blue jumper was loose in row 18."
Circuit works sometimes.Intermittent connection or damaged wire.Inspect wire and connector condition."Battery clip lead moved at connector; output dropped out."
Output is inconsistent.Connector not fully seated.Disconnect source and inspect connector area."Two-pin connector was partially seated; reseated with power off."
Switch has no effect.Wire on wrong switch terminal.Power off, identify common and switched terminal if part is labeled."Switch path did not include center terminal; corrected per instructor diagram."
Output stays off.Return wire missing, loose, or in wrong row.Trace from load back to source negative."Return jumper was one breadboard row above intended row."

Knowledge Check

1. Why does wire color help but not prove a connection?
2. What is one sign of a poor terminal connection?
3. How can a connector fault become intermittent?
4. Why should continuity testing be done with power off?
5. A jumper is in the wrong breadboard row. Which fault category could this create?
Exit ticket: Write one clear technician note about a wire, connector, or terminal issue.

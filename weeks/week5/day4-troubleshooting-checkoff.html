Day 4 Purpose

Students demonstrate that they can troubleshoot safely and systematically. The checkoff evaluates the process, not just the final answer. A student who guesses the fault but cannot explain the symptom, evidence, correction, and verification has not met the technician standard yet.

Safety-critical rule: If a student moves wires, changes parts, or uses continuity/resistance without controlling power first, stop the checkoff, coach the student, and retry with a fresh scenario.

Learning Targets

  • State the observed symptom clearly before changing the circuit.
  • Identify the expected source-control-load-return path.
  • Make the circuit safe before inspection, continuity, resistance, or rewiring.
  • Use visual inspection and one appropriate test to collect evidence.
  • Correct one approved issue at a time.
  • Verify the corrected circuit and complete a repair note.
  • Explain the difference between finding a fault and proving a fault.

Checkoff Flow Students Must Demonstrate

  1. Read or observe the board symptom.
  2. State what the circuit should do if working correctly.
  3. Identify source, control point, load, and return.
  4. Make the circuit safe before handling or continuity/resistance checks.
  5. Inspect visible workmanship: wire seating, polarity, connector fit, exposed conductor, and path layout.
  6. Choose one safe test or inspection step that matches the suspected category.
  7. Record evidence before making a correction.
  8. Correct one approved issue.
  9. Reconnect or test only when safe and approved.
  10. Verify operation and document the result.

Retry and Coaching Notes

If the student misses a safety-critical step, stop immediately, reteach the step, and require a new scenario. If the student guesses correctly without evidence, give a second scenario and require the student to verbalize the troubleshooting flow before correction. If the student struggles with documentation, allow the circuit performance to stand but require a rewritten repair note before final signoff.

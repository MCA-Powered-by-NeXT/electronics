Student Reading: Learn This First

A fault board is a practice circuit with a problem built into it on purpose. The goal is not to “win” by finding the answer quickly. The goal is to practice the troubleshooting process so you can explain how you found the answer safely.

Start by observing the symptom before touching anything. What does the board do? What should it do? Is the LED off, dim, flickering, always on, or only working when something moves? A clear symptom statement keeps you from jumping to a cause too early.

Next, control power. If you are inspecting wires, moving components, using continuity, or using resistance, power should be disconnected in this beginner lab. Do not troubleshoot by wiggling a live circuit. That creates bad habits and sometimes new faults.

Then sketch or trace the expected path. You need to know where the source, control, resistor, load, and return should be before you can find where the path failed. A fault is easier to find when you know what “normal” is supposed to look like.

Collect evidence one step at a time. Visual inspection might reveal a reversed LED or loose jumper. Continuity might reveal an open path. A voltage measurement might show a weak source. Each piece of evidence should answer a specific question.

Fun fact: the fastest troubleshooters often look slow at first because they are thinking before touching. Then they solve the problem faster because they do not create three new problems while chasing the first one.

Before the lab, write this sentence: “My first job is to describe the symptom, not guess the cause.”

Beginner Extra Help: Treat the Board Like a Work Order

A technician work order has a customer complaint, expected operation, inspection evidence, correction, and verification. Your fault board repair ticket is practice for that kind of thinking.

Do not write “fixed wire.” Write what wire, where it was, what symptom it caused, what you changed, and how you verified the result. Future-you should be able to read the ticket and understand the story.

Joke check: “Board was being dramatic” may be emotionally accurate, but it is not a repair ticket. Translate the drama into evidence.

Day 3 Purpose

Students apply the troubleshooting method to controlled low-voltage faults. This lab should feel like a beginner technician work order: receive a board, record the symptom, control power, inspect, test one thing, correct one approved fault, verify operation, and document the work clearly.

Use small circuits similar to the simple examples found in beginner electronics references: battery or trainer source, switch, resistor, LED or buzzer, wire path, and return. The lab does not require advanced theory. It requires patient observation and evidence.

Lab safety: Students use only assigned fault boards. Disconnect power before moving wires, changing components, or using continuity/resistance. Students do not work on non-training power sources or unknown equipment.

Learning Targets

  • Observe and describe a fault symptom before touching the circuit.
  • Use the eight-step troubleshooting method on a controlled board.
  • Choose safe checks that match the suspected fault category.
  • Use visual inspection, continuity, or assigned low-voltage measurement as evidence.
  • Correct one approved fault and verify final operation.
  • Complete a repair ticket clear enough for another technician to understand.

Lab Safety Checklist

  • [ ] Work only on assigned fault board.
  • [ ] Inspect before touching.
  • [ ] Identify source, load, switch/control, and return.
  • [ ] Identify power state before every action.
  • [ ] Disconnect power before moving wires or components.
  • [ ] Use resistance and continuity only with power off.
  • [ ] Use DMM setup checklist before any measurement.
  • [ ] Test one thing at a time.
  • [ ] Correct only instructor-approved faults.
  • [ ] Verify and document final result.

Instructor Coaching Prompts

  • What is the symptom, not the guessed cause?
  • What should the circuit do if it were working?
  • Which section are you testing: source, control, path, load, or return?
  • What evidence did that test give you?
  • What changed after your correction?
  • How would another technician know what you did?
  • What will you do if the first test does not support your guess?

Lab Extension Options

  • Fast finishers: Give a second board with a different fault category and require a shorter repair ticket.
  • Students needing support: Provide a partially completed expected-path diagram and ask them to find only one missing connection.
  • Documentation challenge: Students trade repair tickets and decide whether another person could reproduce the work.
  • Communication practice: Students give a 60-second technician handoff: symptom, evidence, correction, verification.

Knowledge Check and Exit Ticket

1. What symptom did you observe first?
2. What safety step came before changing the circuit?
3. What evidence supported your diagnosis?
4. How did you verify the correction?
5. What would you do if your first test did not support your suspected cause?
Exit ticket: Write one complete repair-ticket sentence including symptom, evidence, correction, and verification.

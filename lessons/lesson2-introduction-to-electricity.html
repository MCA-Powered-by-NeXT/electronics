Lesson 2: Introduction to Electricity

Estimated time: 75-90 minutes. Learn the circuit model that supports every build and troubleshooting activity in the course.

Lesson Purpose

This lesson gives students a simple, reusable way to understand circuits before they start building them. The model is source, path, load, and return. Students will use this model in later weeks to build LED circuits, use a DMM, test continuity, read schematics, and troubleshoot faults.

The goal is not to memorize a definition of electricity. The goal is to look at a simple circuit and explain what must be present for it to work safely.

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the source, path, load, and return in a simple circuit.
  • Explain open circuits, closed circuits, and short circuits.
  • Describe why current requires a complete path.
  • Use a simple circuit diagram to reason about faults.
  • Connect basic electricity ideas to safe troubleshooting habits.
  • Use beginner circuit vocabulary in a short written explanation.

Main Concept: Source, Path, Load, Return

Every working circuit needs a complete route for current. A battery by itself is not a working circuit. An LED by itself is not a working circuit. A circuit works when a source pushes current through a path, through a load, and back to the source through a return path.

Simple LED circuit model:

Source (+ battery) -> Path -> Current-limiting resistor -> LED load -> Return (- battery)

If any required part of the loop is missing, current stops.

This model is simple, but it is powerful. Later in the course, when a circuit fails, students will ask: Is the source present? Is the path complete? Is the load good? Is the return complete?

Rule of thumb: If you cannot trace the full loop with your finger or pencil, do not power the circuit yet.

Open, Closed, and Short Circuits

A closed circuit has a complete intended path. A switch that is turned on creates a closed path. An open circuit has a break somewhere. A switch that is off creates an open path on purpose. A broken wire creates an open path by accident.

A short circuit is different. It is not simply a circuit that does not work. A short is an unintended low-resistance path, often bypassing the load. Shorts can create excessive current and heat.

Closed path:
+ -> switch closed -> resistor -> LED -> -

Open path:
+ -> switch open     resistor -> LED -> -

Short path:
+ -> accidental wire -> -     (load bypassed)
Safety reminder: If a wire, battery, resistor, or LED becomes hot, disconnect the source. Heat is evidence that current may be taking the wrong path or exceeding a component rating.

Why Current Needs a Complete Path

Current is the movement of electric charge through a closed loop. In the water analogy, voltage is the push, current is the flow, and resistance is a restriction. The analogy is not perfect, but it helps beginners see why a break in the path stops the flow.

In a simple battery circuit, current leaves one side of the battery, travels through the load, and returns to the other side. If the return wire is missing, the circuit is not complete even if the positive wire is connected correctly.

Technician Scenario: Dead LED Trainer

Suppose an LED training circuit does not light. A beginner may immediately say, "The LED is bad." A technician uses the source-path-load-return model instead:

  1. Source: Is the battery connected and within the expected voltage range?
  2. Path: Are the jumper wires in the correct breadboard rows?
  3. Load: Is the LED installed correctly and paired with a current-limiting resistor?
  4. Return: Is there a complete path back to battery negative?

This process prevents random part swapping and prepares students for systematic troubleshooting later.

Technician Tips

  • Trace the circuit with your finger before applying power.
  • Do not assume two breadboard holes are connected just because they are close together.
  • Use red for positive and black for return when possible, but verify the actual path.
  • If nothing happens, suspect an open path first. If something gets hot, suspect a short or wrong value.
  • When troubleshooting, describe the symptom before changing the circuit.
  • Use one clear vocabulary set: source, path, load, return. It will make later troubleshooting easier.

Common Mistakes

  • Missing the return path: Students often wire the positive side but forget the path back to the battery.
  • Bypassing the load: A jumper may accidentally connect positive to negative without going through the LED and resistor.
  • Confusing open and short: An open stops current because the path is broken. A short may allow too much current through the wrong path.
  • Powering before inspecting: Inspection should happen before the battery is connected.
  • Assuming the visible part is the fault: The LED may be fine even if it is off; the path may be incomplete.
  • Skipping the resistor: In an LED circuit, the resistor is not optional. It limits current.

Knowledge Check

1. What four parts does every simple working circuit need?
2. What is the difference between an open circuit and a short circuit?
3. In a simple LED circuit, what is the load?
4. Why can a circuit have a battery connected and still not work?
5. What should you check first if a wire or component gets hot?
6. Why is a current-limiting resistor part of the intended path in a simple LED circuit?

Key Takeaways

  • A working circuit needs a source, path, load, and return.
  • An open circuit is an incomplete path; a short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path.
  • Tracing the loop before powering a circuit prevents many beginner mistakes.
  • The source-path-load-return model becomes the foundation for troubleshooting later in the course.

Lesson 6: DMM Measurements and Continuity Testing

Estimated time: 2-3 hours. Practice measurement setup, continuity testing, and simple component checks.

Learning Outcomes

  • Measure approved DC voltage sources.
  • Measure resistor values with power off.
  • Use continuity mode to test wires, switches, and fuses.
  • Record expected value, actual value, unit, and pass/fail result.

Key Vocabulary

Continuity: a complete low-resistance path. Open: no complete path. Closed: complete path. Expected value: what the circuit or part should measure. Actual value: what the meter shows.

Main Concept

Continuity testing is one of the fastest ways to find broken wires, open switches, blown fuses, and missing connections. It is usually done with the circuit powered off so the meter can safely test the path with its own small signal.

Continuity examples: Good wire: probe A ---- wire ---- probe B = beep Open wire: probe A ---- break probe B = no beep Good fuse: beep Blown fuse: no beep
Power off first: Continuity and resistance checks must be done only after the circuit is verified de-energized.

Technician Tips

  • Test the meter by touching probes together before starting continuity work.
  • Move one probe at a time when tracing a path so you do not lose your place.
  • A beep means a low-resistance path exists; it does not prove the part can carry the required load current.
  • Write OL, open, or no beep consistently so your notes make sense later.

Common Mistakes

  • Continuity testing a powered circuit.
  • Confusing a no-beep result with a bad meter without testing the probes together first.
  • Assuming switch terminals without checking which pair opens and closes.
  • Replacing a fuse without asking why it failed.

Knowledge Check

1. Why should continuity be tested with power off?
2. What meter result would you expect from a blown fuse?
3. What information belongs in a measurement log?

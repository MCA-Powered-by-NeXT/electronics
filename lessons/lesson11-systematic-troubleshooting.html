Lesson 11: Systematic Troubleshooting

Estimated time: 2-3 hours. Use evidence instead of guessing.

Previous LessonWeek 5Next LessonRelated LabTroubleshooting Checkoff

Learning Outcomes

  • State a symptom clearly before testing.
  • Use a repeatable fault-finding sequence.
  • Distinguish open faults, short faults, polarity errors, and wrong component values.
  • Verify and document a correction.

Key Vocabulary

Symptom: what the circuit is doing wrong. Fault: the actual cause. Isolation: narrowing the problem area. Verification: proving the fix works. Repair ticket: written record of symptom, tests, fault, correction, and final result.

Main Concept

Good troubleshooting follows a process. Do not replace parts randomly. Start with the symptom, check the power path, check the return path, inspect wiring and polarity, test likely components, correct one fault at a time, and verify the circuit after the change.

Troubleshooting flow: Symptom -> Visual inspection -> Source voltage -> Return/ground -> Control path -> Load -> Correct -> Retest -> Document
Safety first: Disconnect power before rewiring, replacing parts, or continuity testing. Power only after the circuit is inspected and ready.

Technician Tips

  • Write down the symptom before touching the circuit.
  • Measure at the halfway point of a path to divide the problem area.
  • Fix one thing at a time so you know what changed the result.
  • If a fuse opens again, do not simply replace it again; find the cause.

Common Mistakes

  • Calling the first visible damaged part the root cause without testing.
  • Making multiple changes at once and losing the evidence trail.
  • Skipping final verification because the circuit seemed to work once.
  • Failing to record measurements and then retesting everything later.

Knowledge Check

1. Why should the symptom be written before testing?
2. What is one sign of an open circuit?
3. Why is final verification part of the repair?

Learning Outcomes

  • Use V = I x R for simple beginner calculations.
  • Identify AC and DC sources conceptually.
  • Explain battery polarity and safe low-voltage source use.
  • Decode common 4-band resistor color values.

Key Vocabulary

Ohm's Law: V = I x R. DC: direct current, such as a battery. AC: alternating current, such as wall power. Polarity: positive and negative orientation. Tolerance: acceptable resistor value range.

Main Concept

Ohm's Law helps technicians predict current, choose resistors, and check whether a measurement makes sense. In this course, AC is discussed conceptually only. Student labs use safe low-voltage DC batteries or instructor-approved training supplies.

Example: Supply = 9 V Resistor = 470 ohms I = V / R = 9 / 470 = about 0.019 A = 19 mA
No wall outlet measurements: Do not measure wall outlets or unknown AC systems in this course. AC voltage work requires specific training, authorization, and supervision.

Resistor Color Code Quick Method

For a 4-band resistor, read the first two color bands as digits, the third as the multiplier, and the fourth as tolerance. Example: yellow-violet-red-gold = 47 x 100 = 4700 ohms = 4.7 k ohms, plus or minus 5%.

Technician Tips

  • Before powering a circuit, check source voltage and polarity.
  • Choose a resistor that keeps LED current in a reasonable beginner range.
  • Estimate first. If the calculated answer is wildly different from your estimate, check units.

Common Mistakes

  • Using k ohms as if it were ohms without converting.
  • Reading resistor bands from the tolerance end.
  • Forgetting that batteries can sag under load.
  • Connecting a polarized part backwards.

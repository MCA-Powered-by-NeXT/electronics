Lesson 1: Safety and Technician Fundamentals

Estimated time: 75-90 minutes. Learn how technicians work safely, think carefully, and document what they do.

Lesson Purpose

This lesson introduces the habits that make the rest of the course possible. Students will not begin by wiring circuits. They will begin by learning how to approach a workstation, identify hazards, control power, use basic PPE, and think like entry-level technicians.

Safety in this course is practical. It is not a list of rules to memorize and forget. It is a routine students will repeat before measuring, building, troubleshooting, or documenting any circuit. A technician who learns to pause, inspect, and explain their next action is already working more professionally than someone who rushes into a circuit without a plan.

Non-negotiable: Do not work on wall outlets, mains-powered devices, open power supplies, vehicle electrical systems, charged capacitors, or unknown energized equipment. This course uses low-voltage battery-powered DC training circuits only.

Learning Outcomes

  • Describe common entry-level electronics technician duties.
  • Identify basic electrical and lab hazards before work begins.
  • Select appropriate PPE for beginner electronics lab work.
  • Explain ESD awareness and lockout/tagout awareness at a beginner level.
  • Apply a safe work sequence: inspect, de-energize, verify, work, test, document.
  • Explain why documentation is part of safe technical work.
  • Distinguish between personal safety, equipment safety, and measurement reliability.

What Entry-Level Technicians Actually Do

Entry-level electronics technicians often support installation, inspection, testing, maintenance, troubleshooting, repair, and documentation. They may work with training boards, low-voltage devices, production equipment, communication systems, automation panels, or repair benches depending on the workplace.

The specific equipment may change, but the habits stay consistent:

  • Inspect: Look for obvious damage, unsafe conditions, missing labels, loose wires, or incorrect setup.
  • Measure: Use the correct tool and setting to gather evidence.
  • Build: Assemble circuits or wiring paths according to a plan.
  • Troubleshoot: Use symptoms and measurements to narrow down the fault.
  • Document: Record what was found, changed, measured, and verified.
Technician mindset: A technician does not just ask, "Can I make it work?" A technician asks, "Can I make it work safely, explain how it works, and prove that it works?"

Three Kinds of Safety

Beginners often think safety only means preventing injury. That is the most important part, but technicians also protect equipment and protect the quality of their measurements.

Safety TypeQuestion To AskExample
Personal safetyCould this hurt someone?Missing safety glasses while clipping component leads.
Equipment safetyCould this damage a tool, circuit, or component?A jumper wire accidentally shorts the battery terminals.
Measurement reliabilityCould this cause a false or confusing reading?Loose leads or cluttered wiring make it hard to know where probes are touching.

This three-part view helps students give better answers during inspections. Instead of saying, "This bench is messy," a stronger technician statement is, "The loose wires make the circuit path hard to trace and could create an accidental short or false measurement."

Main Concept: The Technician Safety Loop

Before working on a circuit, use a repeatable safety loop. Repeating the same loop reduces mistakes, especially when the circuit does not behave as expected.

Technician safety loop:
1. Inspect the bench and tools
2. Identify hazards
3. Remove or control power
4. Verify the circuit state
5. Perform the task
6. Test the result safely
7. Document what happened

Scenario: A student sees an LED circuit that is not lighting. The unsafe approach is to move wires while the battery is still connected. The technician approach is to disconnect the source, inspect the path, make one change, inspect again, reconnect power, and observe the result.

What students should notice: The safety loop does not slow the work down once it becomes a habit. It prevents repeated mistakes, damaged parts, and unclear troubleshooting notes.

Electricity You Can See: Static vs. Current

Students may have already experienced electricity before entering the lab. A static shock from a doorknob, a balloon sticking to hair, or a spark from clothing are all familiar examples of electrical charge. Those examples are useful, but they are not the same as the current that flows through a working circuit.

TypePlain-Language DescriptionCourse Connection
Static electricityCharge builds up and may discharge suddenly.Helps explain ESD awareness and why sensitive parts should be handled carefully.
Current electricityCharge flows through a complete path.Used in the battery-powered circuits students will build later.

Safe mini-demonstration: The instructor may demonstrate static charge with a balloon and small paper pieces. Students should notice that the effect happens without building a complete circuit. Then compare that to a battery-powered LED circuit, where the LED only works when the path is complete.

Electrical Hazards in Beginner Labs

Most hazards in this course come from poor habits, not high voltage. Students should learn to spot small risks early because those same habits matter in more advanced environments.

HazardWhat It Looks LikeCorrective Action
Short circuitA jumper wire accidentally connects battery positive directly to battery negative.Disconnect power, remove the unintended connection, and inspect before reconnecting.
HeatA resistor, wire, battery, or LED becomes hot.Disconnect power immediately and notify the instructor.
Flying leadsClipped component leads launch when cut.Wear safety glasses and control the lead while clipping.
Damaged insulationA wire has exposed conductor where it should be insulated.Remove the wire from service and replace it.
Wrong meter setupThe meter leads or dial do not match the task.Stop and reset the meter before probing.
Clutter or liquidsTools, loose leads, drink cups, or bags crowd the work area.Clear the bench before beginning work.

PPE and Workstation Expectations

  • Wear safety glasses during labs, especially when cutting or handling component leads.
  • Keep drinks, food, backpacks, and loose metal objects away from the workstation.
  • Use the correct tool for the job; do not strip wire with teeth, scissors, or improvised blades.
  • Keep red and black power wiring consistent when possible, but always verify with the circuit.
  • Report damaged meter leads, cracked parts, hot components, smoke, odor, or unclear instructions.
Why this matters: A clean bench is not about appearance. It makes the electrical paths easier to see and reduces accidental shorts.

LOTO and ESD Awareness

Lockout/tagout awareness means students understand that real workplaces use formal procedures to control hazardous energy. This course does not certify full LOTO practice, but students should know the basic principle: do not work on equipment that can unexpectedly energize, move, heat, spin, or start.

ESD awareness means students understand that static electricity can damage electronic components even when the person does not feel a shock. In this course, ESD is introduced as a good technician habit: handle parts carefully, avoid unnecessary touching of pins, and use instructor-provided ESD controls when required.

Editor note for local customization: Add your program's required PPE, ESD, and LOTO policies here if they differ from this introductory course language.

Technician Tips

  • Stop and ask when the power source is unclear.
  • Disconnect the battery before moving wires or components.
  • Use one hand to steady probes and keep probe tips controlled so they do not slip together.
  • Write down what changed after each test; memory is not documentation.
  • Do not bypass protection devices such as fuses. A blown fuse is evidence, not an inconvenience.
  • When something gets hot, disconnect power first and investigate second.
  • Use the same safety routine every time, even when the task seems simple.

Common Mistakes

  • Changing wiring while powered: This often creates accidental shorts or confusing results.
  • Trusting wire color blindly: Wire color helps, but it is not proof. Verify the connection.
  • Skipping PPE because the circuit is small: Small parts and clipped leads can still cause injuries.
  • Assuming a device is safe because it is unplugged: Some devices can store energy. This course avoids those devices, but the awareness matters.
  • Using the meter before checking the dial and lead jacks: Meter setup must match the measurement.
  • Writing vague notes: "It did not work" is less useful than "LED stayed off; battery disconnected before inspection; return wire missing."

Mini Activity: Hazard Recognition Walkthrough

Review a staged bench, photo, or instructor demonstration. Do not touch anything at first. Identify hazards using this checklist, then explain what you would do before starting work.

  • [ ] Is power connected when it should be disconnected?
  • [ ] Are safety glasses being used?
  • [ ] Are liquids or clutter near the circuit?
  • [ ] Are any wires damaged or exposed?
  • [ ] Are meter leads in the correct jacks?
  • [ ] Are parts hot, discolored, or producing odor?
  • [ ] Is the intended circuit path understandable?

Key Takeaways

  • Safety is a process, not a single warning.
  • Technicians inspect before touching and disconnect power before changing circuits.
  • PPE, ESD awareness, and LOTO awareness are part of professional work habits.
  • Good notes help the next person understand what happened.
  • Safe technicians protect people, equipment, and the quality of measurements.

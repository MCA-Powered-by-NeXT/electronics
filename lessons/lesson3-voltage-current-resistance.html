Learning Outcomes

  • Define voltage, current, and resistance.
  • Explain how resistance limits current.
  • Connect electrical quantities to battery, LED, and resistor examples.
  • Use correct units: volts, amps, milliamps, ohms, and kilo-ohms.

Key Vocabulary

Voltage (V): electrical push or potential difference. Current (I): flow of charge, measured in amps. Resistance (R): opposition to current, measured in ohms. Load: useful part of a circuit. Current limit: a resistance chosen to keep current safe.

Main Concept

Voltage pushes current through a complete path. Resistance limits how much current flows. In a simple LED circuit, the resistor protects the LED by limiting current. Without the resistor, the LED may draw too much current and fail.

9 V battery -> resistor -> LED -> return More resistance = less current Less resistance = more current
Rule of thumb: Convert milliamps to amps before doing Ohm's Law math. 20 mA = 0.020 A.

Technician Tips

  • Measure voltage first when a powered low-voltage training circuit appears dead.
  • Resistance measurements are made with power off.
  • Use units every time you write a measurement. A value without a unit is incomplete.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing voltage with current.
  • Assuming a higher-voltage battery is always better.
  • Writing 20 instead of 0.020 A when calculating with 20 mA.
  • Forgetting that real components have ratings and limits.

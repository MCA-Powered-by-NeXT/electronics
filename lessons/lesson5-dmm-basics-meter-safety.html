Lesson 5: DMM Basics and Meter Safety

Estimated time: 2-3 hours. Learn the meter setup habits that prevent bad measurements and damaged meters.

Learning Outcomes

  • Identify the display, dial, common jack, voltage/resistance jack, current jack, leads, and probes.
  • Select a DMM function before touching probes to a circuit.
  • Explain why incorrect current setup can damage the meter or circuit.
  • Perform a pre-measurement safety check.

Key Vocabulary

Common jack: black lead connection. V/ohm jack: red lead connection for most voltage, resistance, and continuity work. Current jack: special red lead connection for current measurements. Range: expected measurement size. Fuse: sacrificial protection inside many meters.

Main Concept

A DMM is only safe and useful when its jacks, dial setting, and circuit connection match the measurement. Voltage is measured across a source or load. Resistance and continuity are measured with power off. Current measurement is more advanced because the meter becomes part of the circuit path.

Before measuring: 1. What quantity am I measuring? 2. Is the circuit powered or unpowered? 3. Are the leads in the correct jacks? 4. Is the dial on the correct function? 5. Where exactly will each probe touch?
Current mode warning: Never put a meter in current mode directly across a battery or voltage source. Use current mode only with correct jack selection, correct range, meter fuse protection, and instructor approval.

Technician Tips

  • Touch the probes together in continuity mode before using it; this confirms leads and meter response.
  • Start with voltage when checking a powered low-voltage circuit because it tells you if energy is present.
  • Keep probe tips controlled; slipping probes can create shorts.
  • Read the unit on the display, not just the number.

Common Mistakes

  • Leaving the red lead in the current jack after a previous measurement.
  • Trying to measure resistance on a powered circuit.
  • Using AC voltage mode on a DC battery.
  • Writing down a value without the unit.

Knowledge Check

2. Why is current mode riskier than voltage mode?

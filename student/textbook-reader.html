How To Use This Reader

The daily lesson pages tell you what to do in class. This reader explains the concepts behind those activities in a textbook style: what the idea means, why technicians care about it, how it appears in a simple low-voltage circuit, what mistakes beginners commonly make, and what evidence should appear in your notes.

Each day includes a direct link to the matching daily lesson page. Read the textbook section before the activity, then return to the lesson page for the agenda, lab task, checkoff, quiz, or exit ticket.

Course safety boundary: These readings describe electronics concepts using assigned low-voltage classroom examples. Students do not work on wall power, unknown energized equipment, open power supplies, vehicle electrical systems, charged capacitors, or non-training circuits.

Official Source Reference Shelf

These sources support the course's safety and measurement language. They are not substitutes for instructor directions.

  • OSHA Electrical Safety and Health Topics — workplace electrical hazards and safe work practices.
  • OSHA Control of Hazardous Energy — hazardous energy and lockout/tagout awareness.
  • BIPM SI Base Units — official SI base-unit reference, including the ampere for electric current.
  • BIPM SI Brochure — official SI reference for units, prefixes, and derived units.
  • NIST SI Units — U.S. reference for SI units and measurement language.

Week 1: Safety, Technician Habits, and Circuit Foundations

Week 1 Day 1

Safety and Technician Habits

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: Electronics work starts before the circuit is touched. The first technician skill is not wiring, measuring, or repairing. The first skill is deciding whether the work is assigned, low-voltage, inspected, and safe enough to begin.

Electricity can be useful, but uncontrolled electrical energy is a workplace hazard. OSHA identifies electrical hazards such as electric shock, electrocution, fires, and explosions, so even a beginner course should build the habit of stopping, inspecting, controlling the source, and asking when the situation is unclear. In this course, students are not working on facility power or energized workplace equipment, but they are practicing the thinking pattern technicians need later.

When students inspect a station, they should look for the condition of the source, wires, DMM leads, PPE, bench, parts, and instructions. A safe classroom circuit should have an approved low-voltage source, visible wiring, no liquids, no damaged leads, no unknown equipment, and a clear task. If any of those are missing, the correct action is not to continue quietly. The correct action is to stop and ask.

Common mistake: Thinking low voltage means no safety rules. Low voltage reduces risk, but the habit of controlled work still matters.

Week 1 Day 2

Tinkercad Circuits

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: A simulator lets students practice circuit thinking before the parts are physical. The purpose is not to click parts until something works; it is to predict, build, test, observe, and explain.

A simulated LED circuit contains the same core elements as a physical circuit: source, conductor, current-limiting resistor, LED, switch, and return. The simulation lets students see how small wiring choices affect the circuit. A jumper in the wrong row can act like an open path. An LED reversed in the path may not light. A resistor placed next to an LED but not actually in series with it may not protect it.

Simulation is also a documentation tool. Students should write what they expected before running the simulation, what actually happened, and what changed after correction.

Common mistake: Assuming a circuit is correct because it looks neat. Neat layout helps, but the actual electrical connections must be traced.

Week 1 Day 3

Electricity and the Circuit Model

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: A working circuit needs a complete intended path from source, through load, and back to source. If the path is missing, reversed, bypassed, or uncontrolled, the circuit will not behave as expected.

The source provides electrical energy. Conductors provide the path. The load changes electrical energy into light, sound, heat, or motion. The return completes the loop back to the source. This source-path-load-return model is the course foundation.

Open, closed, and short circuits describe path conditions. A closed circuit has the intended complete path. An open circuit has a break. A short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path that may bypass the load or protection.

Common mistake: Tracing only to the load and forgetting the return. The return path is part of the circuit.

Week 1 Day 4

Safety Inspection Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: Many circuit and safety problems can be found by inspection before a meter is used. A careful technician looks first, then measures only when there is a focused question.

An inspection is a planned look at PPE, source, wires, parts, tools, bench condition, labels, and circuit state. Damaged insulation, loose jumpers, unclear power state, liquids, clutter, and wrong meter lead placement are all problems students should recognize before work continues.

Inspection supports measurement accuracy too. If the setup is physically unclear, a meter reading may be misleading. A good inspection note connects condition to consequence: “Loose jumper could open the LED path; disconnect source and reseat before testing.”

Official source connection: OSHA's electrical safety topic page emphasizes hazard recognition and safe work practices.

Week 1 Day 5

Week 1 Review and Readiness

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: Week 1 builds the mental model students need before calculations and meters. Students should understand safety boundaries, circuit loops, expected behavior, and basic documentation.

A student is ready for Week 2 when they can identify source, path, load, and return; explain what a switch does; identify open and short path conditions; and write a useful safety or circuit note.

Review answers should include vocabulary, evidence, and safe next action. “The LED is off” is a symptom. “The LED is off because the return jumper is missing” is a possible cause. “The return row is visibly disconnected” is evidence.

Week 2: Electrical Quantities and Basic DC Circuits

Week 2 Day 1

Voltage, Current, Resistance, and Power

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: Voltage, current, resistance, and power are the core quantities that describe what a circuit is doing.

Voltage is electrical potential difference, or beginner-friendly “push,” supplied by an approved source. Current is the flow of charge through a complete path. Resistance is opposition to current flow. Power describes how quickly electrical energy is converted by a load into light, sound, heat, motion, or another form.

The best beginner explanation is to attach each quantity to the loop: the source provides voltage, the complete path allows current, the resistor limits current, the load uses energy, and the return completes the path. BIPM identifies the ampere as the SI base unit for electric current, and official SI references support using clear units such as V, A, Ω, and W in technical notes.

Common mistake: Saying voltage flows. Use “voltage is supplied or measured across points” and “current flows through a complete path.”

Week 2 Day 2

Resistors and Color Code

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: A resistor is intentional opposition to current. In beginner LED circuits, the resistor protects the LED by limiting current.

Color bands are a compact label system. Students should read the first digit, second digit, multiplier, and tolerance, then connect the value to the circuit job. The resistor usually has no polarity in this course, but its value and placement matter.

If the resistor is not actually in series with the LED, it cannot limit LED current. Physical closeness is not the same as electrical placement. Trace the path to confirm the current must pass through the resistor.

Week 2 Day 3

Ohm's Law and Current Limiting

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: Ohm's Law relates voltage, current, and resistance. The common form V = I × R helps technicians estimate what should happen before building or measuring.

In beginner language, more voltage can push more current through the same resistance, and more resistance can reduce current from the same source. This is why resistor value matters in a simple LED circuit.

Calculations should serve the circuit, not replace it. Students should calculate, inspect, build, observe, and document. The math gives an expected result; measurement and behavior provide evidence.

Week 2 Day 4

Battery Voltage and LED Circuit Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: A battery-resistor-LED circuit contains the full pattern of practical electronics: source, path, protection, load, return, inspection, operation, and documentation.

The battery or approved DC source provides voltage. The resistor limits current. The LED converts electrical energy into light when polarity and current path are correct. The return completes the circuit.

Before power, inspect source approval, resistor placement, LED orientation, jumper rows, return path, and unintended paths. A working LED is useful, but documentation is what turns the activity into technician evidence.

Week 2 Day 5

Week 2 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: Week 2 connects the circuit loop to measurable quantities. Students should explain what voltage, current, resistance, and power mean in a simple low-voltage circuit.

Review should focus on relationships. Current requires a complete path. Resistance limits current. Voltage is measured across two points. Power describes energy conversion by the load. Units belong in every serious technical note.

Students are ready for Week 3 when they can explain what they might measure and why. The meter will provide evidence only when the student chooses the correct function, points, circuit state, and units.

Week 3: Digital Multimeter and Continuity

Week 3 Day 1

DMM Parts and Safety

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: A digital multimeter is useful only when the task, meter setup, circuit state, and probe placement match.

The display shows the reading, but the reading is meaningless without the function and unit. The dial selects the measurement type. The black lead normally goes in COM. The red lead goes in the voltage/resistance jack for this course's voltage, resistance, and continuity practice. Current jacks are special-use.

Voltage, resistance, and continuity are different tasks. Voltage uses an approved powered low-voltage source or circuit point. Resistance and continuity require power off. Current mode requires special series placement and instructor control.

Common mistake: Letting the meter setup from the previous task carry into the next task without checking jacks and dial.

Week 3 Day 2

Voltage and Resistance Measurements

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: Voltage measurement and resistance measurement answer different questions and require different circuit states.

Voltage asks what electrical difference exists between two points. In this course, voltage practice uses assigned low-voltage DC sources or approved trainer points. Resistance asks how much opposition an unpowered part or path has. Resistance is measured with power disconnected.

A complete measurement note includes item, value, unit, circuit state, and meaning: “R1 measured 987 Ω with power off, close to expected 1 kΩ.”

Week 3 Day 3

Continuity, Switches, and Fuses

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: Continuity means a conductive path exists between two named points. Continuity testing is a power-off test.

A beep or low resistance usually means a path exists, but the student must name endpoints and circuit state. Switches intentionally change continuity. Fuses open a path when a fault condition occurs, so an open fuse should be treated as evidence to investigate.

Continuity prepares students for troubleshooting because it can isolate where an unpowered path is complete or open.

Week 3 Day 4

DMM Practical Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: The DMM checkoff assesses process. A lucky reading is not the same as safe meter use.

Students must identify the task, choose correct function and jack, confirm circuit state, control probes, read the value, record units, and explain meaning. A student who uses the wrong jack or skips circuit-state checking needs coaching even if the displayed number looks reasonable.

Retry is focused practice on the missed habit: units, probe control, jack selection, power-off confirmation, or current-mode recognition.

Week 3 Day 5

Week 3 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: Week 3 is about safe evidence. Every meter use should answer a specific question.

If the question is source voltage, use DC voltage. If the question is resistor value, use resistance with power off. If the question is path completeness, use continuity with power off and named endpoints. If the question involves current, stop unless the instructor controls the setup.

Students are ready for Week 4 when they can describe a circuit path before testing it.

Week 4: Components, Schematics, Series, and Parallel

Week 4 Day 1

Components and Wiring

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: Components are physical parts with jobs. Wires and connectors are not just accessories; they are part of the circuit path.

Resistors limit current. LEDs produce light and require correct polarity and current limiting. Diodes conduct more easily in one direction. Switches open and close paths. Fuses open during certain fault conditions. Wires, connectors, and terminals create the path and can create faults when loose or damaged.

Component ID means name, purpose, polarity or handling note, and possible fault.

Week 4 Day 2

Schematics and Tracing

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: A schematic is a map of electrical relationships, not a photograph of physical layout.

Reference labels such as R1, SW1, LED1, and B1 name parts so a technician can discuss and test them clearly. Nodes are shared connection points. Proximity is not connection; electrical continuity is connection.

Students should trace from source through control, protection, load, and return. The return path is part of the circuit.

Week 4 Day 3

Component and Schematic Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: Technician work requires moving between the physical part, the schematic symbol, and the actual electrical connection.

A resistor in a tray, a resistor on a board, and a resistor symbol all represent the same part type, but the technician still asks: What value is it? Where is it in the path? What job is it doing?

Physical-to-schematic comparison is troubleshooting practice. If the physical circuit does not match the schematic, the mismatch becomes evidence.

Week 4 Day 4

Series and Parallel Circuits

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: Series and parallel describe path structure. Series means one path. Parallel means multiple branch paths sharing source and return points.

In series, one open can stop the whole path. In parallel, one branch can fail while another branch still works. This helps troubleshooting because the symptom points toward shared sections or branch-specific sections.

Do not decide series/parallel by appearance. Decide by tracing how many complete paths exist from source to return.

Week 4 Day 5

Week 4 Checkoff and Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: Week 4 combines parts, symbols, tracing, and path structure so students can troubleshoot with evidence in Week 5.

Component identification tells what each part should do. Schematic tracing shows how parts should connect. Continuity reasoning checks whether an unpowered path is complete. Series/parallel reasoning predicts which symptoms match which fault locations.

Students are ready for Week 5 when they can describe intended path, component role, possible open-path effect, and safe test plan.

Week 5: Control, Workmanship, and Troubleshooting

Week 5 Day 1

Control and Workmanship

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: Control devices change whether a path is complete, and workmanship determines whether that path remains reliable.

A switch, pushbutton, connector, relay contact, or jumper can control a load by opening or closing the path. The technician must identify which path is controlled and whether the control affects one load, one branch, or a shared source path.

Workmanship is electrical reliability made visible. Loose conductors, strained leads, hidden connections, wrong terminals, and unlabeled returns create faults that are hard to diagnose.

Week 5 Day 2

Systematic Troubleshooting

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: Troubleshooting is a controlled process for moving from symptom to evidence to correction to verification.

The symptom is what the circuit does. A possible cause is a hypothesis. Evidence is what the technician observes, measures, traces, or compares. A correction changes one thing. Verification proves whether the correction solved the symptom.

Random part swapping hides evidence. Change one thing at a time and write what happened.

Week 5 Day 3

Fault Board Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: A fault board is a controlled practice system. It lets students apply troubleshooting without creating unsafe or unknown conditions.

Common beginner faults include missing jumper, reversed LED, loose connector, open training fuse, wrong resistor row, missing return, weak source, and wrong switch terminals. Each fault should have an expected symptom and a safe evidence path.

A repair ticket should include board ID, symptom, expected operation, evidence, correction, and verification.

Week 5 Day 4

Troubleshooting Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: The troubleshooting checkoff tests whether students can use the process consistently under observation.

Students should be rewarded for controlling power, stating the symptom, comparing expected and actual behavior, collecting useful evidence, correcting one issue, verifying, and documenting. Guessing the fault without evidence needs coaching.

The strongest performance uses technician language: symptom, safety, expected path, evidence, correction, verification.

Week 5 Day 5

Week 5 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: Week 5 prepares students for final projects by teaching them what to do when a circuit does not work on the first try.

Common fault categories include source, path, control, load, return, component value, polarity, connector/terminal, and workmanship. A symptom can have more than one possible cause, so evidence matters.

Day 5 should connect troubleshooting to Week 6 final project planning: define expected operation before building, so troubleshooting has a reference point.

Week 6: Final Project, Documentation, and Workplace Habits

Week 6 Day 1

Final Project Planning

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Big idea: A final project should start as a clear function and an approved plan, not a random collection of parts.

A good project plan names source, control, current-limiting/protection, load/output, return, test method, and evidence of success. Simple projects can show strong technician skill if they are planned, built, tested, troubleshot, documented, and explained well.

Unapproved scope changes create safety and troubleshooting problems.

Week 6 Day 2

Build and Initial Test

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Big idea: Building in stages makes testing and troubleshooting possible.

Build source, control, protection, load, and return in a controlled sequence. Inspect each section before moving on. A project wired all at once is harder to troubleshoot because the student cannot tell where the problem began.

Initial testing can produce working, partially working, not working yet, or unsafe/unclear results. All are useful if documented honestly.

Week 6 Day 3

Troubleshoot, Revise, Document, and Maintain

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Big idea: Final project problems are solved using the same Week 5 troubleshooting process, then documented for handoff.

Students compare the approved plan to the actual build. Differences become possible causes. They record symptom, evidence, correction, and verification. Documentation should happen during the work, not be invented afterward.

Maintenance thinking asks whether the project is ready for future use: wires, labels, connectors, storage, tools, and documentation.

Week 6 Day 4

Final Demonstration

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Big idea: A final demonstration is an explanation of evidence, not just a show that something turns on.

The student should explain project purpose, source, path, control, load, return, protection, safety checks, test evidence, troubleshooting history, and maintenance note. The documentation packet supports the explanation.

Follow-up questions test whether the student understands the circuit and not just the final result.

Week 6 Day 5

Final Quiz, Certificate, and Pathways

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Big idea: The final day connects every course skill into one technician profile: safe work, simple circuit reasoning, measurement evidence, troubleshooting, documentation, and communication.

Students should connect these course skills to real pathways such as industrial maintenance, automation, robotics, telecommunications, renewable energy, electronics repair, manufacturing, and field service. All require safety boundaries, basic circuit understanding, evidence, and communication.

The certificate checklist is a record that the student practiced the essential habits and skills in the course.

Week 2 Official Source Notes

Week 2 introduces measurement language. Students should write values with units. Use official SI references for unit language: BIPM SI Base Units, BIPM SI Brochure, and NIST SI Units.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: Voltage, Current, Resistance, and Power

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by reminding students of the Week 1 circuit loop. A circuit needs source, path, load, and return. Voltage, current, resistance, and power are words used to describe what happens in that loop.

Teach voltage first. Voltage is electrical potential difference. For beginners, call it the electrical push provided by the source. A battery has voltage between its terminals. Later, students will measure voltage across two points.

Teach current second. Current is the movement of electric charge through a complete path. Current does not move through an open path. This is why return matters.

Teach resistance third. Resistance is opposition to current. A resistor provides intentional resistance. Resistance is not always a problem; it is often how a circuit is controlled.

Teach power last. Power describes how quickly electrical energy is converted by a load. An LED converts electrical energy to light. A buzzer converts electrical energy to sound. A resistor may convert electrical energy to heat.

Now attach units. Voltage uses volts, current uses amperes, resistance uses ohms, and power uses watts. Have students write each quantity with its symbol and a circuit example.

End with a prompt: “In a battery-LED circuit, the source provides __, current flows only if __, the resistor __, and the LED __.”

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Resistors and Color Code

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Begin by explaining the job of a resistor. A resistor provides a planned amount of opposition to current. In many beginner LED circuits, the resistor is included to limit current.

Show students that a resistor value is part of the circuit plan. A resistor is not chosen because it looks right. It is chosen because its resistance value helps the circuit operate in the intended range.

Teach the color-code reading process slowly. First find the correct orientation. Then identify the first digit, second digit, multiplier, and tolerance band if present. Students should write the value with ohms, kilo-ohms, or mega-ohms.

Explain tolerance as allowed variation. A resistor marked 1 kΩ may not measure exactly 1000 Ω. This does not automatically mean it is bad. Students should compare expected and measured values later.

Now connect value to placement. A resistor must be in the actual current path to limit current through the LED. If the resistor is nearby but not connected in series, it cannot do its job.

End by having students decode a resistor and write a purpose sentence: “This resistor is __ Ω. In the LED circuit, it is used to __.”

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Ohm's Law and Current Limiting

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start with the relationship, not the formula. Voltage, current, and resistance are connected. Changing one affects what happens in the circuit.

Introduce Ohm's Law as V = I × R. Explain each symbol before solving anything: V is voltage in volts, I is current in amperes, and R is resistance in ohms.

Give students a plain-language version. If voltage stays the same and resistance increases, current decreases. If resistance stays the same and voltage increases, current increases.

Now connect the formula to LED safety. The resistor limits current through the LED. Without the correct resistor in the path, the LED may be too bright, fail, or behave unpredictably.

Teach students to use calculations as predictions. The calculation tells what should happen if the circuit is built correctly. It does not prove the circuit is wired correctly.

After each calculation, students should write an interpretation sentence: “This current estimate is reasonable because __” or “This value needs review because __.”

End with a check: students identify known values, choose what they are solving for, calculate, and explain the result in words.

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: Battery Voltage and LED Circuit Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Begin by describing the circuit before building it. The approved source provides voltage. The resistor limits current. The LED produces light. The return completes the loop.

Tell students to inspect before power. The source must be approved. The resistor must be in the LED path. The LED polarity must be correct. The return must be connected. No jumper should bypass the resistor or load.

Build in a sequence. Place the source connection. Place the resistor path. Place the LED with correct orientation. Place the return. Then inspect the whole loop before applying power.

When the circuit works, students should not stop at “it lights.” They should explain why it lights: the path is complete, the LED is oriented correctly, and current is limited by the resistor.

If the LED does not light, students should follow the loop. Check source, path, resistor, LED polarity, and return. Make one correction at a time.

End with a lab note: source used, resistor value, LED polarity note, expected operation, actual operation, and any correction made.

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Week 2 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Start by reviewing the four quantities in circuit order. Source relates to voltage. Complete path relates to current. Resistor relates to resistance. Load relates to power and energy conversion.

Have students write each unit and what it measures. Volts measure voltage, amperes measure current, ohms measure resistance, and watts measure power.

Review resistor color code as a reading skill. Students should not guess resistor values from memory or color impression. They should read bands and write units.

Review Ohm's Law as a prediction tool. The formula helps students estimate current or resistance, but it does not replace inspection.

Review the LED circuit as a complete example. Students should explain why the resistor matters, why LED polarity matters, and why the return matters.

End by preparing for Week 3. Ask students to write three measurement questions: one for voltage, one for resistance, and one for continuity. These questions will become DMM tasks.

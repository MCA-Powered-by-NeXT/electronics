Official Source Notes for Week 2

Week 2 introduces measurement language. Official SI references matter because technicians must communicate quantities with correct units. The ampere is the SI base unit for electric current, while volts, ohms, and watts are standard electrical units used in technical work. The course expects students to write numbers with units so another person can understand the evidence.

Day 1: Voltage, Current, Resistance, and Power

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Voltage, current, resistance, and power describe circuit behavior. They are not just textbook words. They help a technician explain what a source provides, what moves through the path, what limits that movement, and what the load does with electrical energy.

Voltage can be described for beginners as electrical push or potential difference. A battery has voltage between its terminals. A DMM later measures voltage across two points, not “through” a part in the same way current flows through a path.

Current is the flow of electric charge through a complete path. No complete path means no intended current. This is why the Week 1 return path matters: without return, the loop is incomplete.

Resistance is opposition to current. In beginner circuits, resistance is often intentional. A resistor can limit current so a part such as an LED is not asked to carry more current than intended.

Power describes the rate at which electrical energy is converted. A load may convert electrical energy to light, sound, heat, or motion. Even though the math can wait, the idea matters immediately: parts have limits.

Day 1 is successful when students can connect every quantity to the circuit loop and write the correct unit: volts for voltage, amperes for current, ohms for resistance, and watts for power.

Day 2: Resistors and Color Code

Open the Day 2 lesson page

A resistor is a component made to provide a predictable amount of resistance. In a simple LED circuit, the resistor is a protective and control component because it limits the current in the LED path.

Color bands are a compact label system. Students should read the bands slowly and record the value with units. A number without ohms, kilo-ohms, or mega-ohms is incomplete technician information.

The first two bands often give the first two digits, the third band gives the multiplier, and a fourth band may show tolerance. Tolerance means the measured value may be a little above or below the marked value while still being acceptable.

Resistors in these beginner circuits usually do not have polarity. That means either direction can work electrically. However, placement still matters. A resistor outside the actual current path does not limit current through the LED.

Students should connect resistor value to circuit behavior. A very high value may make an LED dim or off. A very low value may allow too much current for the intended classroom setup. The exact safe values are assigned by the instructor.

Day 2 prepares students for both calculation and measurement. Later, they will compare the marked value to DMM resistance readings and decide whether the difference is reasonable.

Day 3: Ohm's Law and Current Limiting

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Ohm's Law connects voltage, current, and resistance. The common form V = I × R is a way to describe how these quantities relate in a simple resistive circuit.

The formula should not be memorized without meaning. V stands for voltage in volts, I stands for current in amperes, and R stands for resistance in ohms. Students should be able to explain the relationship before solving problems.

For the same resistance, increasing voltage tends to increase current. For the same voltage, increasing resistance tends to reduce current. This is the core idea behind current limiting.

Current limiting is especially important for LEDs. A basic LED circuit usually includes a resistor in series with the LED so current is controlled. The resistor is not a random extra part; it is part of the safe design.

Calculations provide expected values. They do not prove the circuit is wired correctly. A student may calculate a reasonable resistor value but still place the resistor in the wrong breadboard row.

Day 3 should end with students connecting the math to the physical circuit: source voltage, resistor value, estimated current, and the reason the LED needs current limiting.

Day 4: Battery Voltage and LED Circuit Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

The LED circuit lab brings the Week 2 concepts together. The source supplies voltage, the resistor limits current, the LED acts as the load, and the return completes the circuit.

Before applying power, the student should inspect the build. Is the source approved? Is the resistor in the LED path? Is the LED orientation correct? Is the return connected? Are there any jumpers that bypass the resistor or load?

A working LED is useful evidence, but it is not the only evidence. The student should record the source used, resistor value, LED orientation note, expected operation, actual operation, and any correction made.

If the LED does not light, students should use the path model rather than guessing. Possible causes include open path, missing return, reversed LED, wrong row, weak source, missing resistor, or loose jumper.

This lab also introduces the idea of expected versus actual. The expected result is based on the plan. The actual result is what the circuit did. Technician work begins when students compare those two honestly.

Day 4 prepares students for Week 3 because the next step is measurement. The DMM will help only if students already know what they expect the circuit to do.

Day 5: Week 2 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Week 2 review asks students to connect vocabulary, units, calculations, and physical circuit behavior. The goal is not isolated definitions. The goal is circuit explanation.

Students should be able to say that voltage is measured in volts, current in amperes, resistance in ohms, and power in watts. They should also explain what each quantity means in a simple LED circuit.

Students should understand why a resistor is included. The resistor limits current in the intended path. The value matters, and the placement in the path matters.

Students should also understand that labels and calculations give expected information. A resistor color code gives an expected value. Ohm's Law gives an expected relationship. A lab observation gives actual behavior.

Readiness for Week 3 means students can choose meaningful measurement questions. Instead of asking “What do I do with the meter?” they should ask “Do I need to know source voltage, resistor value, or path continuity?”

A strong Week 2 review answer uses vocabulary, units, and path reasoning together: “A 9 V source provides voltage; current can flow only through the complete resistor-LED-return path; the resistor limits current.”

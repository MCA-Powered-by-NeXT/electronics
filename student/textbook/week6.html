Week 6 Source Notes

Week 6 keeps the low-voltage safety boundary while connecting the course to workplace habits. Students should control sources, document evidence, use units, and explain their work clearly.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: Final Project Planning

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by defining the project function before choosing parts. The student should say what the project will do in one sentence.

Next identify the source. The source must be approved for the class. Students should write the type of source and why it is acceptable.

Now identify the control. The control is what starts, stops, or changes the output. It may be a switch, pushbutton, or another simple classroom control.

Identify the load or output. The load might be an LED, buzzer, lamp, or other approved low-voltage output. Students should write what the output will do.

Identify protection or current limiting. If the project uses an LED, the resistor must be part of the LED path. If a fuse or connector is used, the student should explain its purpose.

End by defining proof of success. Students should write how they will know the project works: visual output, sound, measurement, continuity check, or demonstration statement.

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Build and Initial Test

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Start the build in sections. Do not wire everything at once. Build source, control, protection, load, and return in an organized sequence.

After each section, stop and inspect. Check for correct row, secure wire, proper polarity, current-limiting placement, and no exposed conductor.

Before power, compare the build to the plan. If the build does not match the approved plan, stop and ask before continuing.

Initial testing should be calm and documented. The project may work, partly work, not work, or be unsafe to test. Each result is useful if recorded honestly.

If the project does not work, do not rebuild randomly. Write the symptom and choose the first safe check.

End with a build note: what was built, what was inspected, what happened during first test, and what the next step is.

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Troubleshoot, Revise, Document, and Maintain

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start by comparing expected operation to actual operation. The difference between those two statements is the troubleshooting problem.

Make the project safe before touching wiring. Disconnect power before resistance checks, continuity checks, or component changes.

Choose one section to inspect or test. Use the source-control-load-return model to decide whether to begin at the source, control, load, or return.

Collect evidence before correction. Evidence may be a visible wrong row, a loose connector, a voltage reading, or an open continuity path.

Revise one thing at a time. After the change, retest and document whether the symptom changed.

Finish with maintenance thinking. Check labels, wire strain, connector seating, storage, tool cleanup, and whether another student could understand the documentation.

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: Final Demonstration

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Start the demonstration by naming the project purpose. The audience should know what the project is supposed to do before seeing it operate.

Point to the source, control, protection/current limiting, load, and return. Do not only point to the output; explain the full path.

Show the test evidence. This may be a function test, measurement note, continuity note, or troubleshooting record.

Explain one problem solved during the build, even if it was small. A project with documented troubleshooting shows technician thinking.

Answer follow-up questions using evidence. If asked why the LED is protected, point to the resistor in the path. If asked how the repair was verified, describe the retest.

End by stating one maintenance or improvement note for future use.

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Final Quiz, Certificate, and Pathways

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Begin by connecting the course skills. Safety, circuit models, units, DMM use, continuity, schematics, troubleshooting, documentation, and communication all work together.

Review the certificate evidence. Students should be able to name which assignment or checkoff proves each skill.

Connect the final quiz to real practice. A quiz answer about continuity should match how the student actually used continuity with power off.

Ask students to identify their strongest technician habit. It might be inspection, clear notes, careful DMM setup, path tracing, or troubleshooting process.

Ask students to identify the next skill they need. A next step might be more schematic practice, soldering, computer-controlled circuits, mechanical troubleshooting, or a career pathway.

End with a professional reflection: “I can safely __. I can explain __. I need more practice with __. My next pathway interest is __.”

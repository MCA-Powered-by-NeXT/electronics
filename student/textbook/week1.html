How Week 1 Readings Work

Each day below gives students information in the order they should learn it. Read the paragraphs in sequence. After each reading, students should be able to explain the idea, point to an example, and write a technician note before opening the daily activity page.

Official safety reference: OSHA Electrical Safety and OSHA Control of Hazardous Energy. Classroom work stays low-voltage and instructor-approved.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: Safety and Technician Habits

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by telling students that electronics work begins before hands touch parts. The first step is to stop and identify the work situation. Ask: What is the assigned task? What source is present? Is this a classroom training circuit? Is anything unknown, damaged, wet, cluttered, or out of place?

Next, explain that the course uses low-voltage training circuits, but the habits are professional. Low voltage lowers risk, but it does not remove the need for inspection. Students are learning a pattern: stop, inspect, control the source, ask when unsure, and document what was found.

Now walk through the station as if it were a checklist. Look at PPE first. Then look at the source. Then look at wires and leads. Then look at tools, the bench surface, and nearby objects. Students should learn to scan from person to source to circuit to workspace, instead of staring only at the part they want to use.

Introduce hazard language. A hazard note should name the condition, explain why it matters, and describe the safe response. For example, “damaged insulation” is not enough. A useful note says, “The red jumper has damaged insulation; remove it from service because exposed conductor could create accidental contact.”

Connect safety to learning. Students should understand that unsafe or messy conditions also make circuits harder to troubleshoot. A loose wire or cluttered board can look like an electrical mystery, but the first evidence may simply be physical condition.

End the reading by having students write the safety loop in their own words: stop, inspect, identify risk, control the source or condition, ask if unsure, and document. This written loop becomes the first technician habit used all course.

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Tinkercad Circuits

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Begin by explaining that Tinkercad is not a game and not a shortcut. It is a safe practice space for thinking like a technician. Students should use it to predict, build, test, observe, correct, and explain.

Introduce the parts in the simulated circuit one at a time. The battery is the source. Wires and breadboard rows create the path. The switch opens or closes the path. The resistor limits current. The LED is the load that produces light. The return completes the loop back to the source.

Before students run the simulation, have them write what they expect. For example: “When the switch closes, current should have a complete path through the resistor and LED, so the LED should light.” This prediction gives meaning to the test.

When the simulation runs, students compare expected and actual behavior. If the LED lights, they should explain why. If it does not light, they should not randomly move parts. They should trace the source, switch, resistor, LED, and return to find where the path breaks.

Teach common simulation faults as information, not failure. A wire in the wrong breadboard row can create an open circuit. A reversed LED can stop visible output. A resistor placed near the LED but not in the actual path does not limit current through the LED.

End by requiring a technician note. The note should include prediction, result, problem if any, correction, and verification. Example: “Predicted LED would light. It stayed off. Found missing return wire. Added return wire. Retested and LED lit.”

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Electricity and the Circuit Model

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start with the simplest complete circuit sentence: a circuit needs a source, path, load, and return. Students should say this sentence several times and then point to each part in a drawing or simulation.

Explain the source first. A source is the part that provides electrical energy for the circuit. In class, the source is an approved low-voltage battery or trainer. Students should not use unknown sources or wall outlets.

Explain the path next. The path is made by conductors such as wires, breadboard rows, terminals, or traces. The path must actually connect the intended points. Color, neatness, or closeness does not prove connection.

Explain the load. The load is the part doing useful work, such as an LED making light or a buzzer making sound. A load needs a complete path through it and back to the source.

Explain the return last, and emphasize that beginners often forget it. Current does not travel to the LED and disappear. The return completes the loop back to the source. Missing return means the intended path is open.

Now teach path conditions. A closed circuit has the intended complete path. An open circuit has a break. A short circuit has an unintended low-resistance path that may bypass the load or protection. Students should practice identifying each condition in simple drawings.

End with a written explanation: “In this circuit, the source is __, the path goes through __, the load is __, and the return is __. If __ opens, the circuit will __.”

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: Safety Inspection Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Begin by telling students that inspection is a real technical skill. The goal is to collect visible evidence before touching, powering, measuring, or changing anything.

Teach the inspection order. First inspect yourself: PPE, attention, and readiness. Second inspect the source: approved, low-voltage, and controlled. Third inspect wires and tools: no damage, no exposed conductor, no wrong meter lead position. Fourth inspect the bench: no liquids, clutter, loose metal, or hidden hazards.

Tell students that staged props are not meant to trick them. They are meant to train their eyes. A cup near electronics, a damaged wire, a loose jumper, or a cluttered board is a condition that should trigger a technician response.

Students should not simply circle hazards. They should explain why each condition matters. A loose wire might create an open circuit. Damaged insulation might create accidental contact. Clutter might hide the actual circuit path.

Now connect inspection to documentation. The inspection table should include what was seen, why it matters, and what should happen next. Students should avoid one-word answers.

End by having students choose one inspected condition and write a professional sentence: “I observed __. This matters because __. The safe next action is __.”

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Week 1 Review and Readiness

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Begin the review by connecting the week together. Day 1 taught safe habits. Day 2 used simulation. Day 3 introduced the circuit model. Day 4 practiced inspection. Day 5 asks whether students can use all four ideas together.

Have students explain the circuit loop without looking at notes. They should say source, path, load, and return, then point to those parts in a simple circuit.

Next, have students explain three path conditions. A closed circuit has the intended complete path. An open circuit has a break. A short circuit has an unintended low-resistance path. Students should be able to draw or describe one example of each.

Now return to safety. A circuit is not ready for work just because it is simple. It must also have an approved source, visible wiring, safe bench condition, and clear instructions.

Students should write one readiness note that combines safety and circuit reasoning. Example: “This LED circuit is not ready for power because the return wire is missing and the source state is unclear.”

End the reading by previewing Week 2: voltage, current, and resistance will make more sense because students already understand the complete path they describe.

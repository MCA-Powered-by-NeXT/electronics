Official Source Notes for Week 1

Week 1 uses OSHA's electrical safety guidance as the official safety reference point. OSHA identifies electricity as a serious workplace hazard and discusses risks such as shock, electrocution, fires, and explosions. In this classroom course, students stay inside the assigned low-voltage training boundary, but they practice the same habit of hazard recognition and safe work control.

The hazardous-energy idea also matters even though students are not doing industrial lockout/tagout. OSHA's control-of-hazardous-energy materials explain why unexpected energization is dangerous during service or maintenance. For this course, that idea becomes a beginner habit: verify the source, control the source, disconnect before changing wiring, and stop when power state is unclear.

Day 1: Safety and Technician Habits

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Electronics work begins with a pause. A beginner often wants to touch the parts immediately, but a technician first checks the situation. The first question is not “What do I connect?” but “Is this the assigned circuit, is the source approved, and is the setup safe enough to begin?”

Electricity is useful because it can transfer energy and control devices, but that same energy can create hazards when it is uncontrolled. In this course, students are not working on wall power or unknown energized equipment. They are using low-voltage classroom trainers so they can build safe habits before working around more complex systems later.

A technician looks at the entire work area, not just the part in hand. PPE, damaged wires, liquids, clutter, loose metal, unknown power state, poor lighting, and wrong tool setup can all affect safety and reliability. Many electrical problems begin as simple physical conditions that were ignored.

Safety is also a communication habit. A student should be able to describe a hazard clearly: what was seen, why it matters, and what safe action should happen next. “Bad wire” is vague. “Red jumper has damaged insulation and should be removed from student use before the circuit is powered” is useful.

Day 1 connects directly to every later lab. Before a student measures voltage in Week 3, traces a schematic in Week 4, troubleshoots a fault board in Week 5, or demonstrates a final project in Week 6, that student should inspect first. Safety is not a separate unit; it is the first step of the technician process.

A strong Day 1 exit answer should include the safety loop: stop, inspect, identify the risk, control the source or condition, ask when unsure, and document what happened. That habit is more important than memorizing a single rule.

Day 2: Tinkercad Circuits

Open the Day 2 lesson page

A simulator is a safe place to practice circuit thinking. Students can connect a virtual battery, resistor, switch, LED, and wires, then observe what happens without risking damaged classroom parts. The goal is not to click until the LED lights; the goal is to predict, build, test, and explain.

Every simulated circuit should still be treated like a real circuit model. The source provides electrical energy, the wires and breadboard rows form the path, the resistor controls current, the LED is the load, and the return completes the loop. If students cannot point to those parts in the simulation, they will struggle when the circuit becomes physical.

Simulation is especially helpful because beginner mistakes are visible and reversible. A wire in the wrong row can create an open path. A missing return keeps the circuit incomplete. A reversed LED may not light. A resistor that is nearby but not actually in the path does not protect the LED.

Students should learn to compare expected behavior to actual behavior. Before starting the simulation, they should write what they think will happen when the switch closes. After testing, they should record what did happen and what evidence supports the conclusion.

The Tinkercad workflow mirrors technician troubleshooting: inspect the design, predict behavior, run the test, observe the symptom, change one thing, and retest. This is the same structure used later with DMM readings and fault boards.

A strong Day 2 note might say: “Expected LED to light when switch closed. LED stayed off. Found LED polarity reversed in the simulation. Reversed LED and retested; LED lit.” That note shows a prediction, symptom, evidence, correction, and verification.

Day 3: Electricity and the Circuit Model

Open the Day 3 lesson page

The most important beginner circuit model is source, path, load, and return. The source provides electrical energy. The path allows current through conductors. The load changes electrical energy into light, sound, motion, heat, or another output. The return completes the loop back to the source.

This model matters because a circuit is not just a collection of parts. A battery, resistor, LED, and wire on the table do nothing until they are connected into an intended path. A technician must be able to trace that intended path before building or testing.

An open circuit is an incomplete path. A switch can intentionally create an open path when it is off, but a loose wire or missing jumper can create an unwanted open path. Students should learn to ask whether an open condition is intended or unintended.

A short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path. In a beginner LED circuit, a misplaced jumper could bypass the resistor or load. Shorts can create unsafe current or cause the circuit to behave in a way that does not match the drawing.

Closed circuit means the intended path is complete. It does not automatically mean the circuit is correct, safe, or well documented. It only means a path exists. A technician still checks whether that path goes through the intended load and protection.

Day 3 prepares students for schematics and continuity testing later. A schematic is a map of the intended path. A continuity test checks whether an unpowered path is complete. Both skills depend on understanding source, path, load, and return.

Day 4: Safety Inspection Lab

Open the Day 4 lesson page

A safety inspection is a structured look at a workstation before work begins. The technician checks the person, tools, source, wiring, components, and work area. In this course, the inspection is done with safe props and low-voltage training materials, but the habit is a real workplace habit.

Students should inspect for PPE, damaged insulation, exposed conductors, liquids, clutter, unclear power state, misplaced leads, and unlabeled parts. These issues are not random; each one can create a safety problem, a measurement problem, or a troubleshooting problem.

Inspection is also a reliability tool. A loose jumper may not be dangerous, but it can create an intermittent fault. A damaged wire may make a circuit work one moment and fail the next. A cluttered bench can hide wrong connections.

A strong inspection response names both the condition and the consequence. “Cup on bench” is incomplete. “Cup near electronics could spill and damage equipment or create an unsafe work area; remove it before lab begins” is a technician response.

The inspection lab teaches students not to rely only on the meter. A meter is useful when the technician has a question, but many questions begin with visual evidence. If the wire is obviously broken, the first evidence is inspection, not measurement.

Day 4 also prepares students for final project demonstrations. In Week 6, students will need to explain how they inspected their project before applying power. The same inspection language begins here.

Day 5: Week 1 Review and Readiness

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Week 1 review is not just a vocabulary check. It asks whether students have the habits needed for physical electronics work. Students should be able to inspect a station, describe hazards, explain the circuit loop, and document observations.

The circuit model is the central readiness skill. Students should be able to identify source, path, load, and return in a simple drawing or simulator. They should also be able to explain what happens when a path is open, closed, or shorted.

Safety and circuit reasoning belong together. A circuit with an unclear source is not ready for work. A circuit with an unknown return path is not ready for power. A circuit with damaged wire is not ready for measurement.

Students should also be able to write a useful note. A strong note includes the observation, location, meaning, and safe next action. Notes are not busywork; they are how technicians communicate evidence.

Readiness for Week 2 means students can talk about a circuit before calculating anything. Voltage, current, and resistance make more sense when students already understand the loop they describe.

A strong Week 1 review answer might say: “The LED circuit needs the battery as source, wires as path, LED as load, resistor for current limiting, and return to battery negative. If the return jumper is missing, the circuit is open and the LED will not light.”

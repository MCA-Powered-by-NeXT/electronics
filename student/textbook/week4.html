Week 4 Source Notes

Week 4 stays inside the same low-voltage safety boundary. Students should disconnect power before continuity testing, identify parts before using them, and use schematic drawings as communication tools.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: Components and Wiring

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by telling students that components are parts with jobs. A technician does not just recognize shapes; a technician explains what each part does in the circuit.

Teach resistors first. A resistor limits current or creates a known resistance in a path. Students should connect resistor purpose to the LED circuits they built earlier.

Teach LEDs and diodes next. These parts are direction-sensitive. The way they are placed matters because they conduct or operate as intended only with correct orientation in a simple DC circuit.

Teach switches, fuses, wires, connectors, and terminals as path parts. A switch controls whether the path is open or closed. A fuse protects by opening under certain fault conditions. Wires and terminals create the connection points.

Now teach inspection. Students should look for labels, stripes, bands, lead length, flat sides, plus/minus marks, loose terminals, and damaged wire.

End with a component note template: part name, purpose, polarity or handling note, where it appears in the path, and one possible fault.

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Schematics and Tracing

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Begin by explaining that a schematic is an electrical map. It is not a picture of the physical board. The drawing shows how points connect, not where every part sits on the table.

Teach symbols as vocabulary. A resistor symbol means resistor. A switch symbol means a control contact. An LED symbol shows a light-emitting diode. A source symbol represents the battery or approved source.

Teach reference labels. R1 means resistor number one. SW1 means switch number one. LED1 names a specific LED. These labels help technicians talk about parts clearly.

Now teach nodes. A node is a shared electrical connection point. A node may look like a line or dot on paper, a breadboard row in the lab, or a labeled terminal on a trainer.

Have students trace in order: source, control, protection or resistor, load, return. They should not stop at the load; the return completes the circuit.

End with a tracing sentence: “Starting at __, the path goes through __, then __, then __, and returns to __.”

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Component and Schematic Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start by reminding students that a technician moves between three views: the real component, the schematic symbol, and the actual connection.

Give an example with a resistor. The resistor in a parts tray, the resistor on the board, and the resistor symbol on the page all represent the same part type. But the technician still needs value, purpose, and path location.

Teach students to compare physical circuit to schematic one section at a time. First find the source. Then find the control. Then find current limiting or protection. Then find the load. Then find the return.

If the physical build does not match the schematic, students should not just say “wrong.” They should name the mismatch: “LED1 return is connected to the wrong row” or “R1 is not in series with LED1.”

Now connect continuity to the lab. With power off, continuity can check whether two schematic points are connected physically. But the student must know the endpoints before testing.

End with a lab note: physical part, matching symbol, reference label, path location, and whether the physical build matches the drawing.

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: Series and Parallel Circuits

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Begin with the path question: how many complete paths exist from source to return? This question decides whether a circuit section is series or parallel.

Teach series as one path. In a series path, current must pass through each part in order. If one part opens, the whole path can stop.

Teach parallel as branches. In a parallel circuit, more than one branch connects between shared source and return points. If one branch opens, another branch may still operate.

Have students predict symptoms. If all loads in a series path are off, one open anywhere in that path could be the cause. If one parallel branch is off while another works, the fault may be branch-specific.

Warn against appearance-based answers. Two LEDs close together are not automatically parallel. A neat drawing is not proof. Students must trace current paths.

End with a drawing-and-explain task: draw one series path and one parallel branch circuit, then write how an open fault would affect each.

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Week 4 Checkoff and Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Start by reviewing the four skills of the week: identify components, match symbols, trace paths, and reason about series/parallel circuits.

Have students identify a component and explain its job. A correct answer should include purpose, polarity or handling note, and possible fault.

Next have students match a part to its schematic symbol and reference label. The goal is not only matching; the goal is communication.

Now have students trace a source-to-return path on a schematic and then on the physical trainer. The two paths should describe the same electrical relationship.

Finally have students decide whether a section is series or parallel by counting paths, not by guessing from appearance.

End with a readiness sentence: “I can troubleshoot this circuit because I know the intended path, the component jobs, and what would happen if a section opened.”

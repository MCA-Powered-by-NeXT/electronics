Official Source Notes for Week 4

Week 4 continues the course safety boundary from OSHA electrical safety guidance: students inspect, disconnect before continuity work, and use only assigned low-voltage classroom trainers. Measurement language still uses official SI units where values are recorded. The week also introduces schematic communication, which is a technician documentation skill even when the drawings are simple.

Day 1: Components and Wiring

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Components are the physical parts that make a circuit behave in a useful way. A technician does not only recognize a part by shape; the technician connects the part to its job in the circuit. Resistors limit current, LEDs create light, switches open and close paths, fuses protect by opening paths during fault conditions, and wires carry the intended connection.

Wiring is part of the circuit, not just something that holds parts together. A wire has endpoints, a route, and a job. If the wire is loose, damaged, or placed in the wrong breadboard row, the circuit may be open or connected to the wrong node.

Some components are polarity-sensitive. LEDs and diodes must face the intended direction. Some buzzers or capacitors may also have polarity markings. A resistor usually does not have polarity in this beginner work, but its value and location still matter.

Students should learn to inspect component markings before building. Lead length, flat sides, bands, stripes, plus/minus marks, and printed labels are clues. The part's identity should be confirmed before it is placed into a path.

Good wiring supports troubleshooting. If a circuit is built neatly and labels are clear, another student or instructor can trace source, control, load, and return. Messy wiring hides evidence and creates accidental faults.

Day 1 is successful when students can name a component, explain its purpose, identify polarity or handling notes, and describe one likely fault involving that part.

Day 2: Schematics and Tracing

Open the Day 2 lesson page

A schematic is a map of electrical relationships. It is not a photograph of the breadboard. Parts may be physically arranged in one way but drawn in a cleaner way so the technician can understand the intended circuit.

Symbols allow technicians to communicate efficiently. A resistor symbol, switch symbol, LED symbol, source symbol, and fuse symbol each tell the reader what kind of component is present. Reference labels such as R1, SW1, LED1, and F1 identify individual parts.

A node is a shared electrical connection point. On a breadboard, a node might be a connected row. On a schematic, it might be a line, junction dot, or label. Proximity is not connection; electrical continuity is connection.

Tracing means following the intended path. Students should start at the source, move through controls and protection, continue through the load, and finish at return. Stopping at the load is a common beginner mistake.

Schematics also help plan safe measurements. A technician uses the schematic to decide where voltage should be present, where continuity should exist with power off, and which part of the path is shared or branch-specific.

Day 2 is successful when students can explain the difference between physical layout and electrical connection, then trace a complete source-to-return path on both a drawing and a trainer.

Day 3: Component and Schematic Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Day 3 connects three views of the same circuit: the physical component, the schematic symbol, and the actual electrical connection. A technician must be able to move between all three views without losing the meaning of the circuit.

A resistor in a tray, a resistor on a breadboard, and a resistor symbol on paper are different representations of the same part type. However, the technician still needs to know value, path location, and purpose. Identification alone is not enough.

Physical-to-schematic comparison is a troubleshooting skill. If the schematic says the path goes through the switch before the resistor, but the physical circuit bypasses the switch, the mismatch becomes evidence. The student should record the mismatch clearly.

Students should practice explaining a circuit in words. A strong explanation says where the source begins, what controls the path, what limits current, what load uses energy, and how the return completes the loop.

Continuity reasoning supports the lab when power is off. If a schematic shows two points connected, a continuity test can help verify the physical connection. But the student must know the endpoints before testing.

Day 3 is successful when students can match parts to symbols, identify reference labels, trace the physical path, trace the schematic path, and describe whether they match.

Day 4: Series and Parallel Circuits

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Series and parallel describe path structure. In a series circuit, current has one intended path through the loads. In a parallel circuit, current has more than one branch path between shared source and return points.

Series circuits are useful for teaching path dependence. If one part of the series path opens, the entire path can stop working. This is why one loose connection can turn off all loads in a simple series path.

Parallel circuits are useful for teaching branches. If one branch opens, other branches may continue to operate if the shared source and return are still good. This helps students use symptoms to narrow the fault area.

Students should not identify series or parallel by physical appearance alone. Two loads next to each other are not automatically parallel. Two loads far apart are not automatically series. The question is how many complete paths exist from source to return.

Series and parallel reasoning directly supports troubleshooting. If every branch is off, inspect shared source, protection, or return first. If only one branch is off, compare that branch to a working branch.

Day 4 is successful when students can draw, build, predict, observe, and explain a series path and a parallel branch circuit using source-to-return language.

Day 5: Week 4 Checkoff and Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Week 4 review brings together component identification, schematic reading, physical tracing, continuity reasoning, and series/parallel structure. These skills prepare students for Week 5 troubleshooting.

A student should be able to identify common components and explain their purpose. The explanation should include whether the part has polarity, whether it controls current, whether it protects the circuit, or whether it creates the output.

A student should also be able to trace a schematic. The trace should begin at the source and end at return. It should include the control, protection, current-limiting part, and load when present.

The checkoff should not be only a matching activity. Matching a resistor to a symbol is useful, but the stronger skill is explaining what that resistor does in the path and where it appears on the physical trainer.

Week 4 also reinforces safe DMM thinking. Continuity can help verify unpowered paths, but students must disconnect power and name endpoints before testing.

Day 5 is successful when students can explain an intended circuit path well enough that they could later troubleshoot it systematically.

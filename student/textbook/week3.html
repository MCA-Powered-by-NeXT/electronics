Day 1: DMM Parts and Safety

Open the Day 1 lesson page

A digital multimeter is a tool for collecting evidence. It is not a magic answer box. The reading matters only when the student knows the task, the setting, the jacks, the circuit state, and the two points being measured.

The display shows the number and sometimes the unit or range. The dial selects the measurement function. The COM jack normally holds the black lead. The voltage/resistance jack holds the red lead for voltage, resistance, and continuity practice in this course.

The current jack is different. Current measurement requires special setup because the meter must become part of the circuit path. Students do not independently use current mode in this course. They learn to recognize it and stop.

Before every measurement, students should say the task out loud. “I am measuring DC voltage across this approved source” leads to one setup. “I am measuring resistance of this disconnected resistor” leads to another setup.

Probe control is part of safety. Slipping probe tips can short points together or give unreliable readings. Students should identify the two points before touching the probes.

Day 1 is successful when students can complete the setup checklist without rushing: task, power state, black lead, red lead, dial, probe points, units, and meaning.

Day 2: Voltage and Resistance Measurements

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Voltage and resistance are different measurements. Voltage asks about electrical difference between two points. Resistance asks about opposition in an unpowered part or path.

When measuring DC voltage, the meter is placed across two points of an approved low-voltage source or trainer. The source is powered because voltage is present. The student must use the voltage function and correct jacks.

When measuring resistance, the circuit must be unpowered. The meter applies a small internal test signal to estimate resistance. If outside voltage is present, the reading can be wrong and the meter can be damaged.

Expected versus measured is a key technician idea. The label on a battery or resistor gives expected information. The meter gives measured evidence. Students compare the two instead of assuming they must match perfectly.

A complete voltage note names the item, reading, unit, and meaning. A complete resistance note also includes power-off state. “R1 measured 987 Ω with power off, close to expected 1 kΩ” is stronger than “987.”

Day 2 prepares students for troubleshooting because good troubleshooting depends on useful evidence. Poor notes make later decisions harder.

Day 3: Continuity, Switches, and Fuses

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Continuity means that a conductive path exists between two named points. Continuity testing is always a power-off test in this course. It helps identify whether wires, switches, fuses, connectors, or path sections are complete.

A beep or low resistance usually means the path is complete. No beep, OL, or very high resistance often means the path is open. But the result only matters if the student knows exactly which endpoints were tested.

Switches are good continuity examples because their job is to change the path. An open switch should interrupt continuity across the controlled terminals. A closed switch should complete continuity across those terminals.

Fuses are not just ordinary wires. They are protective parts designed to open a path under certain fault conditions. If a fuse is open, the technician records it as evidence and considers why it opened.

Continuity can also reveal intermittent problems. If a reading changes when a wire or connector moves, that movement is evidence. The student should document the condition instead of simply saying “it works sometimes.”

Day 3 prepares students for schematic tracing. A continuity test checks whether the unpowered physical path matches the expected path.

Day 4: DMM Practical Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

The DMM checkoff is a process assessment. The goal is not just to get a number. The goal is to demonstrate that the student can choose the correct setup safely and explain the result.

A student should start by identifying the task. Is this voltage, resistance, continuity, or current-mode awareness? Then the student identifies the correct circuit state. Voltage uses an approved powered source. Resistance and continuity require power off.

Next comes meter setup. Black lead in COM, red lead in the correct jack, dial on the correct function, and probes controlled. If any part of setup is wrong, the reading is not reliable evidence.

Students must also document. A correct number without units is incomplete. A correct number without item and meaning is also incomplete. A technician note should allow another person to understand what was tested.

Retry is part of skill development. If a student misses units, chooses the wrong jack, skips power-state confirmation, or crosses probes, the retry targets that habit.

Day 4 is successful when the student can repeat the safe routine calmly and explain why the routine changes for voltage, resistance, and continuity.

Day 5: Week 3 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Week 3 review is about choosing the correct test for the question. The meter is only useful after the technician decides what evidence is needed.

If the question is whether an approved source has voltage, use DC voltage. If the question is the value of a resistor, use resistance with power off. If the question is whether a path is complete, use continuity with power off and named endpoints.

Current mode remains a stop-and-ask topic. Students should recognize the current jack and current settings so they do not accidentally use them for voltage or continuity work.

Students should also understand negative DC voltage readings. A negative sign may mean the probes are reversed relative to source polarity. It is evidence to interpret, not a reason to panic.

The review should connect measurements to notes. Every answer should include task, setup, circuit state, units, and meaning when appropriate.

Students are ready for Week 4 when they can trace a simple path and decide where a voltage, resistance, or continuity measurement would provide useful evidence.

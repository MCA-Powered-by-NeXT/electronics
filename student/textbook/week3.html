Week 3 Source Notes

Use official SI unit language from NIST/BIPM and course safety habits based on OSHA electrical safety awareness. All meter work remains on assigned low-voltage trainers only.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: DMM Parts and Safety

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by explaining that a DMM does not decide what to measure. The student decides the question first. The meter only gives useful evidence when it is set up for that question.

Identify the parts of the meter in order: display, dial, jacks, leads, and probes. The display shows the reading. The dial chooses the function. The jacks and leads control how the meter connects to the circuit.

Teach the normal lead positions for this course. Black lead goes in COM. Red lead goes in the voltage/resistance jack for voltage, resistance, and continuity practice. Current jacks are special-use and require instructor control.

Now teach circuit state. Voltage checks use approved powered low-voltage sources or points. Resistance and continuity checks require power off. This distinction is one of the most important DMM safety habits.

Have students practice saying the setup before touching probes: “I am measuring __. The circuit should be __. My black lead is __. My red lead is __. My dial is set to __.”

End with a note format: item measured, circuit state, meter setting, reading, unit, and meaning. This prevents number-only notes.

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Voltage and Resistance Measurements

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Begin with voltage. Voltage is measured across two points. In this course, students measure assigned low-voltage DC sources or trainer points only.

Tell students to identify the two points before probing. For a battery, that may be positive and negative terminals. For a circuit point, it may be a test point and return. The points must be named in the note.

Next teach resistance. Resistance is measured with power disconnected. The meter sends its own small test signal, so external power can make the reading wrong or damage the meter.

Teach expected versus measured. A label or color code gives an expected value. A DMM gives measured evidence. Difference does not automatically mean failure; students must decide if the difference is reasonable.

Have students write one voltage note and one resistance note in complete form. Example voltage note: “Battery pack measured 6.2 V DC across source terminals.” Example resistance note: “R1 measured 986 Ω with power off.”

End by having students explain the difference: voltage uses powered approved points; resistance uses unpowered components or isolated paths.

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Continuity, Switches, and Fuses

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start by defining continuity as a complete conductive path between two named points. The key words are complete, conductive, and named points.

Teach that continuity is a power-off test. Students must disconnect or verify power is off before checking wires, switches, fuses, or path sections.

Show how to name endpoints. Do not write “wire good.” Write “continuity from switch output to resistor input is present with power off.” The endpoints give the result meaning.

Use switches to show changing continuity. A switch open should interrupt the path. A switch closed should complete the path across the correct terminals. Record the switch position every time.

Use fuses to teach interpretation. An open fuse is not just a failed part. It is evidence that the path opened, possibly because of a fault. The student should record it and ask why.

End with a continuity note template: endpoints tested, circuit state, meter result, and meaning.

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: DMM Practical Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Begin by telling students that the checkoff tests process, not luck. A correct-looking number is not enough if the setup was unsafe or unexplained.

Review the four station types: voltage, resistance, continuity, and current-mode awareness. Students should know what circuit state each station requires before touching probes.

At the voltage station, students should identify approved source, jacks, DC voltage setting, probe points, reading, unit, and meaning.

At the resistance station, students should verify power off, identify the resistor, use correct jacks and function, read the value, and compare to expected.

At the continuity station, students should name endpoints, verify power off, read beep/low resistance/open result, and explain what it means.

At the current-mode station, students should stop and explain why current mode is special-use. Recognition is the skill; independent current measurement is not the task.

End by having students rehearse one complete checkoff explanation aloud before performing it.

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Week 3 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Start by sorting questions, not meter settings. Ask: What do I need to know? Source voltage? Resistor value? Whether a path is complete? Whether current mode is unsafe?

For voltage questions, choose DC voltage, approved powered low-voltage points, correct jacks, and controlled probes.

For resistance questions, choose resistance, power off, and assigned component or isolated part.

For continuity questions, choose continuity or low resistance, power off, and named endpoints.

For current-mode questions, stop and ask. Students should recognize current jacks and settings so they do not accidentally use them for voltage.

End with a review note: “The question is __, so the correct meter task is __. The circuit should be __. The result should be recorded with __.”

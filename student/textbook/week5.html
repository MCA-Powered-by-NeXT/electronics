Official Source Notes for Week 5

Week 5 uses troubleshooting as a safety and evidence process. OSHA electrical safety concepts continue to support source control and hazard recognition. Students still work only on low-voltage trainers, but they practice workplace-style communication: symptom, expected operation, evidence, correction, verification, and documentation.

Day 1: Control and Workmanship

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Control devices decide when a path is complete. A switch, pushbutton, connector, jumper, or relay contact can allow or interrupt current through the intended load. The technician must identify which path is controlled and what symptom would appear if that control failed.

Workmanship is the physical quality of the build. Good workmanship makes a circuit easier to inspect, test, repair, and explain. Poor workmanship can create faults even when the schematic idea is correct.

Loose wires, strained leads, wrong terminals, poorly seated connectors, exposed conductors, and unlabeled returns are not small details. They can create opens, shorts, intermittent operation, and confusing measurements.

Wire color helps communication, but color alone does not prove connection. Students should trace endpoints and record what the wire actually connects. A red wire in the wrong row is still wrong.

Workmanship also affects final project quality. A project that works once but has loose parts or hidden paths may not be reliable. Technicians build for repeatable operation.

Day 1 is successful when students can inspect a build, name a workmanship issue, explain the likely electrical effect, and propose a safe correction.

Day 2: Systematic Troubleshooting

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Troubleshooting is a process for moving from symptom to evidence. The symptom is what the circuit does or does not do. A possible cause is a hypothesis. Evidence is what the technician observes, measures, traces, or compares.

A systematic process keeps the student from guessing. The process usually begins with observation, safety, inspection, expected operation, isolation, test, correction, verification, and documentation.

Changing many things at once is a common beginner mistake. If the circuit starts working after five changes, the student does not know which correction solved the fault. One change at a time preserves evidence.

Expected versus actual thinking is essential. The technician first states what should happen, then describes what actually happened. The difference between the two guides the troubleshooting plan.

Troubleshooting should use the least risky and clearest evidence first. Inspection often comes before measurement. Continuity is used with power off. Voltage is used only on assigned low-voltage points.

Day 2 is successful when students can write the troubleshooting flow and use it on a scenario without jumping directly to part replacement.

Day 3: Fault Board Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

A fault board is a controlled training circuit with a planned problem. The instructor plants a safe low-voltage fault, and the student practices finding evidence without creating unsafe conditions.

The board should have an expected operation. For example, an LED should light when the switch closes. The symptom might be no light, intermittent light, wrong brightness, or switch not controlling the load.

Common safe faults include missing jumper, reversed LED, loose connector, open training fuse, wrong resistor row, missing return, weak source, and wrong switch terminals. Each fault teaches a different part of the troubleshooting process.

The student should not begin by asking, “What part is bad?” A better question is, “What section of the path should I check first based on the symptom?” This keeps troubleshooting tied to circuit reasoning.

A repair ticket is the written evidence trail. It should include board ID, symptom, expected operation, safety step, evidence collected, correction, and verification. The ticket matters because another technician should understand what happened.

Day 3 is successful when students troubleshoot a controlled fault safely and write a repair ticket that shows reasoning instead of guessing.

Day 4: Troubleshooting Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

The troubleshooting checkoff evaluates the process under observation. A student who guesses the fault correctly but cannot explain the evidence still needs coaching. The process is the skill.

The student should begin by stating the symptom. Next, the student controls the source and identifies expected operation. Then the student inspects and chooses a focused test or comparison.

Evidence should be specific. “It is broken” is not evidence. “No continuity between switch output and resistor input with power off” is evidence. “Battery measured acceptable voltage” is evidence.

Correction should change one thing. After the correction, verification proves whether the circuit now behaves as expected. Verification is not optional because a repair without verification is incomplete.

Documentation should be short but complete. The checkoff note should include symptom, evidence, correction, and verification. It should also include safety-critical actions such as disconnecting power before continuity testing.

Day 4 is successful when students can troubleshoot calmly, safely, and clearly enough that another person could follow their reasoning.

Day 5: Week 5 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Week 5 review connects workmanship, control, troubleshooting, and repair documentation. These are the habits students need before independent final project work.

Students should understand fault categories: source, control, path, load, return, component value, polarity, connector or terminal, fuse/protection, and workmanship. These categories help organize possible causes.

A symptom can have multiple possible causes. An LED that does not light could have a weak source, open switch path, missing resistor path, reversed LED, open return, or wrong row. Evidence separates possibilities.

Students should also understand why verification matters. A correction that appears to work once may not be reliable if the original cause was intermittent or if another fault remains.

Week 5 prepares students for Week 6 because final projects often do not work perfectly on the first attempt. The troubleshooting process gives students a safe path forward.

Day 5 is successful when students can write a troubleshooting plan and a repair-ticket sentence that uses symptom, evidence, correction, and verification.

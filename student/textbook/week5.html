Week 5 Source Notes

Week 5 uses OSHA-style safety habits in a low-voltage training setting: control the source, inspect before touching, test only with correct circuit state, and document evidence.

Day 1 Mini-Chapter: Control and Workmanship

Open the Day 1 lesson page

Start by defining control. A control device changes whether a circuit path is complete. A switch, pushbutton, connector, or jumper can control when a load receives current.

Have students identify the control in a simple circuit. Ask what happens when it is open and what happens when it is closed. The answer should be about the path, not just the output.

Now define workmanship. Workmanship is the physical quality of the build. A circuit can be correct in theory but unreliable if wires are loose, terminals are weak, or paths are hidden.

Teach students to inspect workmanship as evidence. A loose connector can create intermittent operation. A strained lead can fail later. A poorly routed wire can hide a wrong connection.

Explain that color coding helps but does not prove connection. Students must trace endpoints and check that the wire actually connects the intended points.

End with a workmanship note: condition observed, likely electrical effect, and safe correction.

Day 2 Mini-Chapter: Systematic Troubleshooting

Open the Day 2 lesson page

Begin by defining the symptom. The symptom is what the circuit does or does not do. “LED off” is a symptom. “Bad LED” is a possible cause, not evidence.

Next define expected operation. Students must know what should happen before they can explain what is wrong. Expected operation comes from the schematic, plan, or instructor description.

Teach the troubleshooting order: observe symptom, make safe, inspect, compare to expected path, choose one test, collect evidence, correct one issue, verify, and document.

Warn against guessing. Replacing parts without evidence can hide the real problem and waste time. Changing several things at once makes it impossible to know what fixed the circuit.

Teach evidence examples: visible loose wire, measured source voltage, open continuity path with power off, wrong resistor row, reversed LED, or switch terminal mismatch.

End with a repair-ticket sentence frame: “Symptom was __. Evidence showed __. Correction was __. Verification showed __.”

Day 3 Mini-Chapter: Fault Board Lab

Open the Day 3 lesson page

Start by explaining that a fault board is a safe training system with a planned fault. The instructor knows the fault; the student practices finding evidence.

Students should begin with expected operation. What should the board do if it works? Which source, control, load, and return are involved?

Next students observe the symptom. They should describe exactly what happens: no output, dim output, intermittent output, switch not controlling output, or unexpected behavior.

Then students make the circuit safe before touching wiring or using continuity. Safety is part of troubleshooting, not a separate step.

Students choose a likely section to inspect or test. For example, if all output is off, start with source or shared return. If one branch is off, compare that branch to a working branch.

End with repair ticket documentation. The ticket should be specific enough that another technician could understand the symptom, evidence, correction, and verification.

Day 4 Mini-Chapter: Troubleshooting Checkoff

Open the Day 4 lesson page

Start by telling students what is being graded: process. The goal is not to guess quickly. The goal is to troubleshoot safely and explain the evidence.

Students should speak the process aloud. First state the symptom. Then state expected operation. Then control power and inspect.

Next, students choose one test or inspection target. The test should match the question. Continuity is power-off. Voltage is only on assigned low-voltage points. Visual inspection is often first.

After collecting evidence, students make one correction. One correction at a time protects the evidence trail.

Then students verify. Verification means the circuit now performs the expected operation, not just that the student changed something.

End with the checkoff note. It should include symptom, expected operation, evidence, correction, and verification.

Day 5 Mini-Chapter: Week 5 Review

Open the Day 5 lesson page

Start by reviewing fault categories. A problem can involve source, control, path, load, return, polarity, component value, connector, terminal, fuse, or workmanship.

Have students connect symptoms to possible categories. If every load is off, shared source or return may be likely. If only one branch is off, branch wiring or load may be likely.

Review why evidence matters. Many possible causes can produce the same symptom. Evidence narrows the list.

Review repair tickets as communication. A repair ticket is not a story; it is a clear record of symptom, evidence, correction, and verification.

Connect Week 5 to Week 6. Final projects often fail on the first attempt. Students should use the same troubleshooting process instead of restarting randomly.

End with a final readiness sentence: “When my project does not work, I will first __, then __, then __.”
